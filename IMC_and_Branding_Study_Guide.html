The thread that ties it together

Everything in the course maps onto one cycle of value. Module I is about communicating value; Module II is about creating and sustaining brand value. The governing rule: branding must be engineered into the marketing mix, not bolted on afterward.

Identify value→ Create value proposition→ Communicate value→ Deliver value→ Extract value→ Measure value→ Sustain value

Value is created for two parties at once: for the customer (satisfaction leading to loyalty) and for the company (price, image, and ultimately brand equity).

S2

Thinking Outside the Wine Box

Franzia & the Mekanism "Franz for Life" campaign: how to pick a creative and an influencer
★ tests: creative selection, advertising vs branding

Franzia is an inexpensive, historically uncool boxed wine that rarely worked with big agencies. To modernize, it partnered with Mekanism on the tongue-in-cheek "Franz for Life" campaign, leaning into the box-wine identity with humor and pop culture. Your job: pick which storyboard and which influencer should run next (version 2.0).

The central lesson: choosing creative is NOT a gut decision

Most managers treat picking an ad as "you know it when you see it." The case argues the opposite: the best brand-building companies make creative selection a systematic, multi-dimensional, teachable process. The literal translation of strategy into an ad is always a disaster; picking the right one is an act of interpretation of the implicit meaning the audience will build.

Brand filters

Is the expression of brand symbolism apt?

Brand filters

Will the ad resonate with the target culture?

Comms filters

Story

Engaging, simple, original, creative? Product woven in naturally?

Comms filters

Product

Campaign filters

Campaign extension

Does it effectively extend equity already built?

Persuasion models (and why branding breaks them)

AIDA: Awareness → Interest → Desire → Action. Hierarchy of Effects: learning → feeling → doing (cognitive → affective → behavioral). These assume a one-way funnel that intervenes at one decision point. Branding rarely works that way; it aims at the value proposition, embedding cultural meaning while consumers co-create the story.

Branding = 4 types of value creation

  1. Build a quality reputation / benefits
  2. Build perceptions that the company is a good relationship partner
  3. Frame how consumers perceive the product experience
  4. Imbue the brand as a symbol, with values and identities

Context: Mekanism is an offbeat creative agency (founded 2003, Jason Harris CEO from 2012). Franz for Life (2019) was a strategic shift for The Wine Group, which usually spends little on advertising. The toolkit is notable for a low-budget brand: influencer marketing, media partnerships, merchandising, and social media rather than expensive TV.

Module I · Reading
S2b

Influencer Marketing

The influencer pyramid and why small can be powerful (ISTMO reading)
★ tests: the pyramid, the reach vs conversion trade-off

Influencer marketing means promoting a brand through people the audience already admires. As budgets shift to social media, where the audience chooses what to watch and trusts the messenger, creators get more valuable, partly because, unlike ads, they essentially can't be skipped or blocked.

The influencer pyramid (by audience size)

Nano1K to 10K · highest authenticity and engagement; seen as ordinary "super-users."
Micro10K to 100K · niche experts with engaged communities.
Macro100K to 1M · wide reach; need ~2 to 5% engagement; sold by post or CPM.
Mega / Celebrity1M+ · massive reach; used mainly for brand awareness.
↑ more reach  ·  ↓ targeting, control, and conversion

The central trade-off: the more followers, the bigger the reach, but the harder to target a group, control responses, and drive purchases. Nano and micro convert better because the recommendation feels authentic, a genuine tip from a peer.

Influencers are also sorted by content vertical: fashion, makeup, foodies, gamers, entertainment, travel, fitness. Matching vertical and audience to the brand is what makes the partnership credible. Thresholds per Conde & Casais (2023) and Korzynski et al. (2025).

Module I · Integrated Marketing Communications
S3

Glossier

Co-creating a cult brand with a digital community: social commerce
★ tests: CLV/CSV math, 8 Cs, CBR, the $52M decision

Glossier grew out of a blog into the first socially driven beauty brand: products developed through crowdsourced community feedback, a friendly brand-as-peer personality, and a direct-to-consumer model that lets it own customer data and curate its community.

The three equity concepts (know the difference)

Brand Equity

The value of the brand to the firm; the differential (better) response consumers give a product because it carries the brand.

Customer Equity

The combined Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) of all customers in the portfolio.

Customer Social Value (CSV)

A customer's peer impact on others' purchasing via social-commerce sharing.

CLV = (Gross Profit per Year × Years in Lifetime) − Customer Acquisition Cost
CLSV (Customer Lifetime Social Value) = CLV + CSV

The 8 Cs framework (building Customer Social Value)

Content → Conversations → Connections → Community → Crowdsourcing → Co-Creation → Consumer-to-Consumer Social Commerce → Club / Cult.

Fournier's Consumer-Brand Relationships (CBR)

Brand relationships behave like human relationships: functional (serve a purpose), multiplex (multi-layered, resemble real human bonds), and dynamic and complex (they evolve). Close "friends" vs casual "acquaintances" generate very different expectations and profitability. Glossier's type is friendship.

The decisions on the table

Where should Glossier invest its $52 million?

Assess each option against control over the Consumer-Brand Relationship. The DTC model gives the most control to curate and manage the community; wholesale (Sephora, 40-50% margins) hands frontline relationship management to a partner.

Maximize CLV and CSV: P2P customer sales reps or paid professional influencers?

Most existing customers are high-CLV but low-CSV. To lift CSV you either recruit a core of existing customers as peer-to-peer sellers (authentic, on-brand, but may lack skill or desire) or use professional influencers (skilled, scalable, but less authentic to a peer-trust brand).

Should Glossier launch a customer loyalty program?

Weigh it against the friendship relationship: a transactional points program can clash with brand-as-peer. The stronger move rewards advocacy and CSV-generating behavior, not just repeat spend.

Vulnerabilities (a likely exam list): profitability, scaling, competition, the DTC ceiling, and a limited target market. A beloved community does not automatically make a scalable business. Tech is core: about one third of staff work in technology, building social commerce as an anti-Amazon model.

Module I · Integrated Marketing Communications
S4

Brands & People: Brand Coolness

Rebranding & relifting: can coolness be engineered? (Warren, Batra, Loureiro & Bagozzi, JM 2019)
★ tests: the 10 characteristics, positioning gap, Harley vs BMW

Almost every brand wants to be cool; few are. The question: can coolness be deliberately engineered and managed, from both the brand's and the agency's point of view (including how AI is reshaping agencies)? It applies in Team Assignment 2: propose a brand name, logo, slogan, and storytelling through a coolness lens.

Two views of any brand

Strategic positioning

  • The image the company intends to project.

Perceived positioning

  • The image consumers actually hold.

The gap between the two is the central thing a brand manager works to close.

The 10 characteristics of brand coolness

01

Rebellious

Opposes, subverts, or combats conventions and norms.

02

Extraordinary / Useful

Sets the brand apart through superior functional value.

03

Aesthetically appealing

Attractive, visually pleasing.

04

Original

Different, creative, does things not done before.

05

Subcultural

Tied to a group seen as outside the mainstream.

06

Popular

Fashionable, trendy, liked by most.

07

Authentic

True to its perceived essence or roots.

08

Energetic

Enthusiasm, energy, vigor.

09

High Status

Prestige, sophistication, esteem.

10

Iconic

Widely recognized as a cultural symbol.

Two routes to cool (the classic examples)

Harley-Davidson became cool because an outlaw-biker subculture adopted it, lending it a rebellious, iconic image. BMW is cool for the opposite reason: it became a widely admired status symbol. Same outcome, two completely different routes. Coolness usually starts in a small niche (subcultural, rebellious, authentic) and some brands then get adopted by the mass and become popular and iconic.

Note the built-in tension: subcultural pulls against popular, rebellious against high status. Cool lives in the balance, which is why it erodes as a brand scales. Rebranding / relifting refreshes a brand's identity to regain relevance without throwing away hard-won equity, the same balancing act as extending a campaign.

Module II · Branding Strategies
S5

Genesis of a Green Brand in the Mayan Rainforest

Building brands with social responsibility (Chicza, the only organic biodegradable gum)
★ tests: brand strategy steps, positioning weaknesses, team exercise (6M by country)

Omar Roquet must build the brand story, identity, and go-to-market strategy to connect rainforest gum (chicle) producers to the best target market. This is the conceptual spine of Module II: brands are cultures that circulate in society as conventional stories, authored by four creators.

Designing a brand strategy (6 steps)

  1. Identify the goals branding can address
  2. Map the existing brand culture (customers/prospects, influencers, current strategy across reputation, relationship, experience, symbolism inferences)
  3. Analyze competition and environment with the Ohmae 3C model (Company, Customer, Competition) to spot branding opportunities
  4. Design the branding strategy (existing culture → drivers → goal, executed through brand engineering)
  5. Implement it
  6. Evaluate on four measures: behaviors, attitudes, relationships, equity

Why classic positioning statements fail

What it captures

  • Target group and need
  • Frame of reference (perceptual + competitive)
  • One differentiating benefit + reason to believe
  • Brand character

Where it breaks

  • Ignores that the brand has a history and a culture
  • Doesn't isolate the 4 components of brand value
  • Assumes only one point of difference; ignores points of parity
  • No forward-looking growth platform; yields vague strategy

The team exercise

Promote the Mexican organic gum in an assigned country.

Deliver: cover page, project background (company, product, brand name, country justification), 4P decisions (Product / Pricing / Channels / Communication), and a communication campaign designed with the 6M framework.

Branding & ethics (4 conditions for benevolent branding)

  1. Companies and consumers have equal information about the product
  2. They have equivalent sophistication in how branding works
  3. Consumers are not over-reliant on heuristics
  4. The authors of the branding effort are revealed

For a green brand this matters doubly: false sustainability claims (greenwashing) violate these conditions. Closing thesis: start with the WHY; purpose matters even more for a social or green brand. Build purposeful brands with social impact, but make them profitable and global.

S6

United Airlines

Brand crisis & reputation management: Dr. Dao dragged off Flight 3411
★ tests: crisis plan, online complaining quadrants, reputation dimensions

In 2017 United overbooked a flight and had aviation security forcibly drag a paying passenger, Dr. David Dao, off the plane. A bystander's video went viral within hours. Brand crisis management is the process of dealing with a disruptive, unexpected event that threatens the organization and its stakeholders.

The timeline (why each day made it worse)

  • Apr 9 Chicago Aviation Security agents forcibly remove Dr. Dao; the video spreads globally.
  • Apr 10 CEO Oscar Munoz apologizes only for having to "re-accommodate" customers, widely seen as tone-deaf.
  • Apr 11 United changes policies; share price drops sharply (down 3.72% intraday).
  • Apr 12 Public demands Munoz resign.
  • Apr 13 Dao's daughter says no one had contacted her father.

Diagnosis: a bad initial response, poor and late communication, and overall mishandling.

Four questions to frame any crisis

Impact on the brand

How deep, how lasting?

Scale of the incident

How far does it spread?

The company's reaction

Anticipate the likely response and its risks.

Who is responsible?

Brand responsibility vs third party.

Online public complaining (Tripp & Grégoire, 2011)

Two axes: understanding (customer actions) vs managing (company actions), and before vs after the complaint. Four quadrants: (1) why customers complain online; (2) how long they hold a grudge; (3) how a company should respond after; (4) how to prevent it.

Reputation: 6 dimensions, 20 attributes

Social Responsibility

Good causes, environment, community.

Emotional Appeal

Feel good, admire/respect, trust.

Vision & Leadership

Market opportunities, leadership, clear future.

Products & Services

Quality, innovation, value, stands behind.

Financial Performance

Outperforms, profitable, low-risk, growth.

Workplace Environment

Fair rewards, good place to work, good employees.

Crisis Management Plan (the checklist)

Prepare a procedures manual; investigate thoroughly; do not lie ("I don't know" beats invention); take it seriously; monitor for early warning signs; do not argue publicly (address people directly); value the criticism; do not take it personally; choose your battles; ask for help. And prepare a war room.

Stat to remember: 70% of customer loss is from deficient service (vs 15% relocation, 10% competition, 5% the contact died). And 95% of dissatisfied customers never complain, they just leave.

S7

Nike & Colin Kaepernick

Brand activism: "Believe in something. Even if it means sacrificing everything."
★ tests: brand activism, FIT, MROI metrics, accretive vs dilutive

Differentiation has evolved: Functional (1900s-1950s) → Emotional / ego-expressive (1960s-1990s) → Moral (1990s-present), where brands "take a stand." In 2018 Nike made Kaepernick, polarizing for kneeling during the anthem to protest racial injustice, the face of its "Just Do It" anniversary campaign.

The decision

Has using Kaepernick been positive or negative for the Nike brand? Which metrics matter most or least?

Use the exhibits. Nike's core customers skew young, urban, diverse: 56% of Black consumers were more likely to buy, 37% of 18-34s, while older and conservative segments skewed negative but are a smaller share of Nike's base. The campaign drove massive earned media; sales and stock recovered and grew. Net: accretive for Nike's actual target despite polarized overall opinion.

The three FIT dimensions

Brand – Cause FIT

Does the cause align with what the brand stands for?

Cause – Company FIT

Does the company have credibility on this issue?

Spokesperson – Cause FIT

Is the messenger an authentic voice for it?

Kaepernick scored high on all three for Nike's athlete-empowerment identity.

Marketing ROI (CFO vs CMO evidence)

A CFO wants financial proof (sales, stock, market cap); a CMO wants brand-equity proof (awareness, engagement, liking, consideration, ad recall). The case maps metrics to MROI implications: earned media impressions, brand engagement, brand knowledge, brand liking, purchase intent, share of voice, brand value, cultural resonance. Tools include word-cloud sentiment analysis of social posts.

The 6 P's of Brand Activism

Purpose (driven by brand values), Policy (advocates real change), People (employees believe in it), Power (use company power to improve the world, not just sell), Publishing (use brand stories and touchpoints as a call to action), Pop Culture (connect with social movements to stay relevant).

Definition: brand activism is brands developing actions to make the world a little better, a response to people's concern for social problems. Done well it makes consumers feel heard, motivates employees, and builds loyalty and social impact. Five types: at-work, political, environmental, economic, legal.

Module II · Branding Strategies
S8

Grupo Britt

Brand architecture & growth strategies: Costa Rican coffee goes global, the multilocal brand
★ tests: brand architecture, the 4 growth alternatives, multilocal, capabilities

Britt started as a garage coffee roaster (1990) and grew into a travel-retail empire of "Britt Shops" in airports across Latin America. Its leader, Pablo Vargas, rose from intern to CEO. The culture prizes intrapreneurship (anyone with an idea can bring it forward), flat hierarchy, first-name basis, and environmental concern, and it built its own e-commerce tech early. The decision: how to grow from about $133M (2015) toward about $200M (2021)?

Brand architecture & the "multilocal" concept

Brand architecture is how a company organizes and relates its portfolio of brands and sub-brands. Britt's distinctive idea is being multilocal: not a generic global brand, but one that feels authentically local in each country (selling local coffee, crafts, and flavors as a sense-of-place souvenir) while running on shared systems behind the scenes.

The four growth alternatives (Ansoff-style menu)

A

Expansive

Open Britt shops in new countries and airports. Needs airport-authority relationships.

B

Intensive

Add more shops within airports it is already in. Deeper penetration.

C

In-Depth

Increase cross-selling, e.g. push products into supermarkets. Brand stretch.

D

Disruptive

Invent new travel-retail formats.

Challenges group into administrative (SKU control, diversification), operational (stiffer competition, limited airport space), and logistics / technology (complex logistics, tech maintenance).

The lesson: growth is not just opportunity. Each path forces you to build new organizational capabilities. Cross-reference the 7 expansion avenues in the frameworks section; Britt is where they get applied to a real decision. Brand strategy itself splits into targeting (allocate resources across segments) and positioning (product, pricing, communication).

Module II · Branding Strategies
S9

Tequila Patrón

Brand authenticity & global markets: selling Mexican-ness back to Mexico
★ tests: authenticity, ultra-premium, grassroots marketing; also the Final Project brief session

Patrón launched in Los Angeles in 1989, when tequila was a cheap shot-drink. Its strategy: create an entirely new "ultra-premium" category by teaching consumers to sip and taste tequila like a fine spirit, emphasizing raw-material quality and an artisanal process. The dilemma: how to position Patrón in Mexico itself, where it was viewed as an "American tequila" and its authenticity questioned.

How the product builds authenticity

Authenticity is grounded in craft. Patrón ages in different barrels (new French oak, used American bourbon oak, Hungarian oak) and offers a line from unaged Silver to Reposado (rested, about 3 to 5 months) to Añejo (aged, about 12 to 14 months), plus the tahona-process Roca and triple-distilled Gran Patrón. Aging follows Mexico's CRT norms (rested 2 to 12 months; aged 1 to 3 years; ultra-aged 3+ years). Blending ensures consistency, so the artisanal story is real, not just marketing.

The threats and the response

Two pressures: the rise of mezcal (production up about 140% in 2012 to 13), which out-authenticities tequila in Mexican eyes, and winning the large 25 to 40 middle and upper-class segment without cheapening the brand. The response leans on digital, trade, and influence marketing plus education at points of contact (bars, restaurants, celebrities).

What is grassroots / guerrilla marketing, and why does Patrón rely on it?

Patrón says it has "never paid a penny" for celebrity endorsements; the brand spreads organically through word-of-mouth and unconventional, low-cost tactics. Grassroots marketing builds awareness from the ground up: slower and cheaper than mass advertising, but the loyalty it generates is genuine and long-lasting.

The authenticity equation

Authenticity is about closing the gap between expectation and reality: if your brand promises authenticity, you had better deliver (HBR 2014). People judge from experience, so the message must match the organization's reality. Main tools to build it: consumer experience, public relations, points of sale, communication, and distribution channels. This is also the Final Project brief session (Círculos de Sabor), built with the 6M framework.

Module III · Brand Love Workshop
S10-13

Brand Love (Dr. Aaron Ahuvia)

How and why people fall in love with products, and the ethics of it
★ tests: 3 personification mechanisms, the "happy accident," intrinsic rewards

A four-session workshop by Dr. Aaron Ahuvia (University of Michigan-Dearborn), a leading researcher on the psychology of brand love. The framing is a three-act story: Act I, boy finds love (people really do love products); Act II, boy loses love (why almost all brands fail to be loved); Act III, boy gets love back (the mechanism that makes brand love possible).

The core thesis: brand love is a "happy accident"

Love is a psychological system that evolved to make families, to bond humans to other humans. It was never designed for products. So when we "love" a brand, we are accidentally firing human-bonding machinery at a commercial object. The practical implication: to build brand love, you must understand human love first.

Why so few brands are loved (Act II)

In Ahuvia's survey of 720 consumers, for every brand loved by more than 100 people there were over 250 brands loved by only a single person. Companies wrongly picture one spectrum from hate to love and assume maximizing quality moves you up it. But quality and trust only get you a reliable product, "the difference between a keeper and a fling." They are necessary, not sufficient. You also need intrinsic rewards: the brand has to make you feel good in itself ("I love how I feel when I use this"), not merely be useful.

The mechanism: Personification (Act III)

Products can only be truly loved once the mind treats them as people, not objects. Three routes:

01

Anthropomorphism

The brand itself seems human (talking to Siri, a "face" on a watch, reluctance to switch off a talking toy).

02

Connecting

The brand becomes part of another person you love (a perfume your mother wore), so love transfers to the object.

03

Self-integration

The brand becomes part of your own identity. Once it is part of the self, you apply self-serving bias and excuse its flaws.

Segmentation for messaging

Ahuvia maps consumers on cultural capital × economic capital (High Society, BoBo / bourgeois-bohemian, Middle Class, Blue Collar), each with distinct media, retailers, and lifestyle signals, so you craft norms-based messaging that fits the group's identity.

The ethics (Session 13): "Don't be the devil incarnate"

Capitalism done right is a positive-sum game: you win by making others better off. The danger is temptation. Older economic theory assumes people rationally weigh costs, benefits, risk, and timing (a personal "discount rate" for sooner vs later rewards), but real consumers can be tempted into self-destructive bargains. Ethical marketers move from an attention economy to an attachment economy, building genuine, healthy love rather than exploiting impulses.

Module IV · Innovation & Branding in the Era of AI
S14

GümmyLife

Entrepreneurial branding: from TikTok fame to a scalable brand
★ tests: scaling, attention vs loyalty, capabilities, the 4-way strategic choice

GümmyLife is a young Mexican vitamin-gummy (nutraceutical) startup founded by three entrepreneurs. It exploded through TikTok-native branding and a creator/affiliate sales model. In January 2026 the founders must define a five-year plan amid a 2026 Mexican tax reform, intensifying social-commerce competition, and an increasingly sophisticated supplement market.

The business model

A blend of TikTok-native branding, the creator economy (sales commissions), performance marketing, AI-enabled personalization, AI-enabled social commerce, and a DTC affiliate system: a platform / ecosystem strategy rather than a simple product company.

The strategic choice

To keep growing sustainably, which direction?

(a) stay digital-first, (b) expand into physical retail, (c) diversify the product line, or (d) expand internationally. Evaluate each with the Entrepreneurial Brand Scaling Framework and a Strategic Assessment Framework.

Three lessons that travel beyond gummies

  1. Attention is not loyalty. Going viral is not the same as building repeat, committed customers.
  2. Algorithmic visibility accelerates growth but does not guarantee sustainability. You do not own the algorithm.
  3. Growth changes the capabilities a company must build. The skills that made you viral are not the skills that run a retail supply chain or an international operation.

Market context, five nutraceutical trends: personalization; demand for scientifically-backed functional ingredients; transparency as a competitive must; convenient product formats; sustainability and ethical sourcing.

Core framework

Brand Value: 4 components

A brand = stories, images, associations built around the product. The four value types appear in Green Gum, United, and the positioning-statement critique. Brand cultures shape each one.

Reputation Value

Stories shape perceived quality of product features. Consumers trust you and feel good buying from you.

Relationship Value

Stories imply the firm is a long-term partner that attends to customer interests.

Experiential Value

Stories frame and shape the experience of the product.

Symbolic Value

Stories imbue the product with values and identities.

Study tool

The Case Method Playbook

A step-by-step way to apply every framework, in order, to any case you are handed. Work top to bottom. Each step names the tool and the question it answers.

Frame the decision

Write one sentence: what decision must be made, and by whom? Is it a communications problem (what to say) or a branding problem (what to be)? Note constraints.Tool: none yet · Output: a crisp problem statement

Situation scan with 3C

Run Ohmae 3C. Customer: who and what do they value? Company: strengths, assets, current equity? Competition: who else competes and how positioned? Add context (culture, regulation, tech, channels).Tool: Ohmae 3C · Output: a one-paragraph situation read

Diagnose the brand with Keller

Place the brand on the Resonance Pyramid. The level where it is weak is usually the real problem to fix.Tool: Keller pyramid · Output: which level is broken

Read the relationship and the culture

Use Fournier: is the bond functional, emotional, identity-based? One-way or a two-way community? Is the brand still pushing one-way persuasion or embedding cultural meaning?Tool: Fournier CBR + AIDA-to-meaning shift · Output: relationship type and cultural fit

Set the objective and the message

Fill in the 6M's. Message and Mission must trace back to the pyramid level from step 3.Tool: 6M's · Output: a filled-in campaign brief

Generate creative and partner options

Run the Franzia 6 creative filters on each idea; use the influencer pyramid to pick partners by reach vs authenticity. Treat creative as structured, not a gut call.Tool: Franzia filters + influencer pyramid · Output: 2-3 evaluated options

Stress test fit and authenticity

For activism, apply Nike's 3 FIT dimensions. For premium or origin brands, apply the Patrón authenticity test. Reject options that fail fit or feel inauthentic.Tool: Nike 3 FIT + Patrón authenticity · Output: options that survive

Choose the growth or positioning move

Use Britt's 4 growth alternatives to name the move; use the coolness lens (strategic vs perceived positioning, niche to mass) to decide how broad. Make one clear recommendation.Tool: Britt growth grid + coolness positioning · Output: one recommended move

Plan execution, risk, and metrics

Sequence the rollout, then pre-plan for trouble using United's crisis principles. Close by stating the metrics from your 6M Measurement so the recommendation is testable.Tool: United crisis principles + Measurement · Output: rollout, risk plan, success metrics

Exam tips: name the framework as you use it (graders reward explicit tool use); diagnose before you prescribe; pick one recommendation and defend it, do not hedge with three equal options; end every answer with measurement so it is testable.

Cheat sheet: most likely on the exam

The frameworks worth being able to draw from memory. Good raw material for the 1-page (both sides) course summary.

  • Keller Resonance Pyramid: Salience → Performance/Imagery → Judgements/Feelings → Resonance.
  • 6M's of IMC: Mission, Market, Medium, Message, Money, Measurement.
  • Franzia creative filters: Benefits, Symbolism, Resonance, Story, Product, Campaign Extension.
  • Influencer pyramid: nano → micro → macro → mega (reach up, conversion/control down).
  • Glossier: 8 Cs + CLV/CSV/CLSV + Fournier CBR (functional, multiplex, dynamic) + 5 vulnerabilities.
  • Coolness: 10 characteristics; strategic vs perceived positioning; Harley (subculture) vs BMW (status).
  • Green Gum: Ohmae 3C + 6-step branding process + 4 ethics conditions + 4 creators of brand culture.
  • United: crisis-management principles + online-complaining quadrants + reputation 6 dimensions.
  • Nike: 3 FIT dimensions + 6 P's of activism + 5 activism types + accretive/dilutive effects.
  • Britt: 4 growth alternatives (Expansive / Intensive / In-Depth / Disruptive) + multilocal.
  • Patrón: ultra-premium category creation + authenticity equation + grassroots marketing.
  • Ahuvia: 3 personification mechanisms (anthropomorphism, connecting, self-integration) + happy-accident thesis + intrinsic rewards.
  • GümmyLife: attention is not loyalty; visibility does not equal sustainability; growth changes capabilities.

Study tool

Quiz yourself

Pick an answer to see if you are right and read the explanation. Works independently of self-test mode.

01Which set correctly lists the 6 M's of IMC?
Mission, Market, Medium, Message, Money, Measurement.
02In the Franzia framework, "Resonance" sits in which filter group?
Resonance is a brand filter (will the ad resonate with the target culture?), alongside Benefits and Symbolism.
03As an influencer's follower count rises, what happens?
Bigger audiences mean more reach but less targeting precision, less control over responses, and weaker purchase conversion.
04Fournier describes consumer-brand relationships as:
Functional, multiplex (resemble human relationships), and dynamic and complex.
05CLSV equals:
Customer Lifetime Social Value = CLV + Customer Social Value (peer impact on others' purchases).
06Why is BMW "cool" (vs Harley-Davidson)?
BMW is cool as a popular status symbol; Harley became cool via an outlaw-biker subculture. Two routes, same outcome.
07Ohmae's 3C model analyzes:
Company, Competition, Customer, used to identify branding opportunities (Step 3 of the branding process).
08A core principle of United's crisis plan is:
Never deny or dismiss, "I don't know" beats inventing facts; monitor early and address people directly.
09Which is NOT one of Nike's 3 FIT dimensions?
The three are Brand-Cause, Cause-Company, and Spokesperson-Cause.
10Britt's "In-Depth" growth alternative refers to:
In-Depth = increasing cross-selling (brand stretch into channels like supermarkets).
11The 3 mechanisms of personification (Brand Love) are:
Anthropomorphism (brand seems human), Connecting (part of a person you love), Self-integration (part of your identity).
12Ahuvia's central thesis is that brand love is:
Love is a psychological system evolved for people and family, only accidentally applied to brands.
13GümmyLife teaches that attention and loyalty are:
Attention is not loyalty; algorithmic visibility accelerates growth but does not guarantee a sustainable business.
14Which vehicle is strongest for behavioral (action-triggering) goals?
Advertising serves cognitive goals, Personal Selling affective, Sales Promotions behavioral.
15What share of the final grade is the final exam?
Final Exam 40%; then Participation 20%, Project 20%, Team Assignments 10%, Quizzes/Exercise 10%.

Reference

Glossary

The terms most likely to show up as definitions or quiz items, with the session that introduced each.

Integrated Marketing Communications S1
Fostering awareness, knowledge, interest, and purchase by bringing together multiple modes of communication in a consistent, complementary way.
Value Creation Cycle S1
Identify, create, communicate, deliver, extract, measure, sustain value, for both customer and company.
AIDA Model S2
Awareness, Interest, Desire, Action. A persuasion model that assumes intervention at one decision point.
Creative Decision Filters S2
Brand (benefits, symbolism, resonance), communication (story, product), campaign (extension). Replaces gut decisions.
Influencer pyramid S2b
Nano, micro, macro, mega by audience size; reach rises while targeting, control, and conversion fall.
Brand Equity S3
A measure of the brand's value to the firm; the differential consumer response to products.
Customer Equity / CLV S3
The combined Customer Lifetime Value of all customers. CLV = (gross profit/year × years) minus acquisition cost.
Customer Social Value (CSV) S3
A customer's peer impact on others' purchasing. CLSV = CLV + CSV.
Social Commerce S3
Restructuring commerce to insert peer-to-peer influence at every stage of the journey.
Consumer-Brand Relationship (CBR) S3
Fournier: brand relationships are functional, multiplex (human-like), dynamic, and complex.
Brand Coolness S4
A measurable quality across 10 characteristics; balances opposing traits (subcultural vs popular).
Strategic vs Perceived Positioning S4
The image the company intends vs the image consumers hold; managers work to close the gap.
Brand Culture S5
The brand as a conventional story authored by companies, popular culture, customers, and influencers.
Ohmae 3C Model S5
Company, Customer, Competition: the strategic triangle for spotting branding opportunities.
Brand Crisis Management S6
Dealing with a disruptive, unexpected event that threatens the organization and its stakeholders.
Reputation Management S6
Managing how the brand is viewed across 6 dimensions and 20 attributes.
Brand Activism S7
Brands developing actions to make the world better; moral differentiation, governed by the 6 P's.
FIT (activism) S7
Brand-cause, cause-company, and spokesperson-cause alignment determining credibility.
Marketing ROI (MROI) S7
Returns from marketing; the CFO weighs financial proof, the CMO weighs brand-equity proof.
Brand Architecture S8
How brands and sub-brands in a portfolio are organized and related.
Multilocal Brand S8
A brand that feels authentically local in each market while running on shared systems.
Brand Authenticity S9
Closing the gap between expectation and reality; a primary driver of brand support.
Grassroots / Guerrilla Marketing S9
Organic, low-cost, word-of-mouth promotion that builds genuine, long-term loyalty.
Personification S10-13
Treating a brand as a person via anthropomorphism, connecting, or self-integration; the route to brand love.
Intrinsic Rewards S10-13
Feeling good in the use itself, not just utility; necessary (beyond quality and trust) for brand love.
Attention vs Attachment economy S10-13
The ethical shift from exploiting impulses to building genuine, healthy love.
6M Framework S5 / S9
Mission, Market, Medium, Message, Money, Measurement: the communication-plan tool.
