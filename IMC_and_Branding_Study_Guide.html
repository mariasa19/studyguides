What is this course?

A graduate (MBA-level) marketing course at IPADEMarketing Management III: Integrated Marketing Communications & Branding, by Dr. Silvia Cacho-Elizondo. It teaches, through real company cases, how firms communicate with the market and how they build brands that people know, trust, and even love. This guide reconstructs the whole course so you can follow it even if you never attended.

The thread that ties it together: the Value Creation Cycle

Everything maps onto one cycle: identify value → create a value proposition → communicate value → deliver value → extract value → measure value → sustain value. Module I focuses on communicating value; Module II on creating and sustaining brand value.

Module I, Integrated Marketing Communications (Sessions 1 to 3)

How brands say things in a world where consumers talk back. Covers the shift from one-way persuasion (AIDA) to embedding cultural meaning, the 6 M’s planning framework, how to select creative and influencers systematically (Franzia), and how to build a cult brand and community online (Glossier).

Module II, Branding Strategies (Sessions 4 to 9)

How brands are built, defended, and grown: engineering coolness; building with social responsibility (Green Gum); surviving a crisis (United Airlines); taking a stand through activism (Nike & Kaepernick); structuring and growing a portfolio (Grupo Britt); and defending authenticity in global markets (Tequila Patrón).

Brand Love Workshop (10 to 13) & Entrepreneurship (14)

Dr. Aaron Ahuvia explains the psychology of why people love brands (personification) and the ethics of it, then the course closes with how an entrepreneur scales a viral idea into a real brand (GümmyLife).

If you only memorize two things: Keller’s Brand Resonance Pyramid (how a brand is built in the mind, from Salience up to Resonance) and the 6 M’s of IMC (Mission, Market, Medium, Message, Money, Measurement). Both appear across nearly every session.

Introduction: Foundations
Module I · IMC · Strategic & critical marketing thinking
▼

What this course is really about

This is a graduate marketing course (Marketing Management III at IPADE) about two intertwined ideas: Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC)how a company coordinates everything it "says" to the market, and Brandinghow a company builds an identity and emotional meaning around its products. The premise is that in the modern world a brand is not just a logo; it is, in the professor's words, a kind of story that circulates in society, co-written by the company, popular culture, customers, and influencers.

The old way vs. the new way of communicating

Traditionally, marketers thought of advertising as psychological persuasion, captured by the classic AIDA model: you grab a consumer's Attention, spark Interest, create Desire, and drive Action (purchase). It assumes a one-way "company talks, consumer listens" funnel. The course argues this is no longer enough. In the new communication paradigm, consumers talk back and talk to each other, through social media, reviews, blogs, and video platforms, so brands no longer fully control their own message. Advertising's deeper job today is to embed cultural value into the brand so it becomes meaningful in people's lives.

Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid (the master framework)

This is the model you'll see again and again. It describes how a brand is built in the consumer's mind, level by level, like climbing a pyramid. You cannot reach the top without securing each level below it:

  • 1. Salience"Who are you?" Does the customer even know the brand exists and what category it's in? This is basic awareness and recognition.
  • 2. Performance & Imagery"What are you?" Performance = how well the product actually works (functional quality). Imagery = the intangible associations, personality, and social meaning of the brand.
  • 3. Judgments & Feelings"What about you?" Judgments are the rational opinions customers form (quality, credibility, relevance). Feelings are the emotional reactions the brand triggers.
  • 4. Resonance"What about you and me?" The peak: an intense, active loyalty and personal attachment, where customers feel a real relationship with the brand and become advocates.
Why it matters: most brands are stuck near the bottom (people know them but feel nothing). The whole point of branding strategy is to climb toward Resonance.

The 6 M's, a checklist for planning any campaign

When you design an integrated communication campaign, you answer six questions, each starting with M:

  • Missionwhat is the objective? (build awareness, change perception, drive sales?)
  • Marketwho exactly are we targeting?
  • Mediumwhich channels reach them best?
  • Messagewhat is the single idea we want to land?
  • Moneywhat's the budget?
  • Measurementhow will we know if it worked?
Running team exercise: throughout the term, teams develop a marketing-mix and communication campaign for an organic Mexican chewing gum, each team assigned a different country, forcing you to adapt branding and messaging to a specific culture.

Creative Selection & Communication Agencies
Module I · IMC · Case: Franzia wine / Mekanism agency, "Franz for Life"
▼

The situation

Franzia is an inexpensive American boxed wine, historically uncool, low advertising budget, and a brand that rarely works with big agencies. To modernize its image it partnered with a creative agency (Mekanism) on a tongue-in-cheek campaign called "Franz for Life," which leaned into the box-wine identity with humor and popular culture. The managerial problem the case puts you in: you are handed several proposed ads (storyboards) and several proposed influencers, and you must decide which creative and which influencers to choose for the next wave of the campaign (version 2.0).

The key lesson: choosing creative is NOT a gut decision

Most managers believe picking an ad is intuitive, a "you know it when you see it" judgment based on experience. The case argues the opposite: the best brand-building companies treat creative selection as a systematic, multi-dimensional, teachable process. They train managers to evaluate creative against explicit filters rather than personal taste.

The Creative Decision Filters (6 filters, 3 groups)

You assess any proposed ad by running it through three layers of filters:

  • Brand Filtersdoes it serve the brand? Benefits: is the framing of the brand's benefits compelling? Symbolism: is the brand's symbolism expressed aptly?
  • Communication Filtersdoes it communicate well? Resonance: will it resonate with the target's culture? Story: does it weave the product naturally into a story (rather than bolting the product on)?
  • Campaign Filtersdoes it work as a campaign? Product: is the execution engaging, simple, original, creative? Campaign Extension: does it effectively extend the equity already built by the existing campaign?

Related ideas

The case also teaches the advertising campaign lifecycle (a campaign matures and eventually needs refreshing, hence "Franz for Life 2.0"), the challenges of extending a campaign without losing what made the original work, and the role of popular culture as raw material for advertising. Franzia's promotional toolkit is notable for a brand with little money: influencer marketing, media partnerships, merchandising, and social media rather than expensive TV.

From the Wrap-Up: matching the vehicle to the goal

Different communication vehicles serve different jobs: Advertising is strongest for cognitive goals (awareness/knowledge), Sales Promotions for behavioral goals (trigger action), and Personal Selling for affective goals (build relationship). Their effectiveness also shifts across the product life cycleadvertising dominates at Introduction/Growth, sales promotions and personal selling matter more at Maturity/Decline.

Nano-influencers (ISTMO article)
Module I · Reading · The influencer pyramid & why small can be powerful
▼

What influencer marketing is

Influencer marketing means promoting a brand through people the target audience already admires for the content they post on Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, or Facebook. As ad budgets shift toward social media, where the audience chooses what to watch and trusts the person delivering the message, these creators become increasingly valuable, partly because, unlike traditional ads, they essentially can't be "skipped" or blocked.

The influencer pyramid (by audience size)

  • Nano-influencerssmallest following, but extremely high trust; seen as ordinary people / "super-users."
  • Micro-influencersniche experts with engaged communities.
  • Macro-influencerslarge reach; the historical foundation of the industry; typically need a minimum engagement (~2 to 5%) and sell by number of posts or CPM (cost per thousand impressions).
  • Mega-influencers / celebritiese.g., a Kim Kardashian; over a million followers; used mainly to boost brand awareness.
The central trade-off: the more followers an influencer has, the bigger the reach, but the harder it is to target a specific group, control how people respond, and actually drive purchases. Nano/micro influencers convert better because their recommendation feels authentica genuine tip from a peer, not a sales pitch.

Influencers are also categorized by content vertical: fashion, makeup, foodies, gamers, entertainment, travel, fitness. Matching the influencer's vertical and audience to the brand is what makes the partnership credible and aspirational.

Storytelling & Digital Brand Communities
Module I · IMC · Case: Glossier, building a cult beauty brand online
▼

The situation

Glossier is a beauty company that grew out of a blog and became the first "socially driven" beauty brand: instead of dictating products from a corporate lab, it crowdsources feedback from its community and co-creates products with them. It sells direct-to-consumer (DTC) and built a passionate, almost cult-like digital community. The case explores how a brand turns a community into both an emotional asset and a business model, and where that model gets fragile.

Three value concepts you must distinguish

  • Brand Equitythe value of the brand to the firm; at the micro level it comes from the different (better) responses consumers give to a product because it carries that brand.
  • Customer Equitythe combined Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) of all the firm's customers. Roughly: CLV = (gross profit per year × years as a customer) − cost to acquire the customer.
  • Customer Social Value (CSV)an addition to CLV that captures a customer's peer impact: how much their recommendations drive other people's purchases. In a community-driven brand, a customer who evangelizes is worth far more than their own spending suggests.

Why people bond with brands, Susan Fournier's research

Fournier showed that consumer-brand relationships behave like human relationships. They are functional (serve a purpose), multiplex (multi-layered, resembling real human bonds), and dynamic & complex (they evolve over time). Different relationship types, close "friends" vs. casual "acquaintances", generate different expectations and very different profitability.

Brand communities & social commerce

A brand community is a group of consumers who bond around a brand; managers can leverage that bond to create value for the firm. Social commerce is the next step: restructuring the buying process so that peer-to-peer (consumer-to-consumer) influence is inserted at every stage of the customer journey, discovery, evaluation, purchase, and post-purchase, through crowdsourcing and shared opinions.

Glossier's vulnerabilities (a likely exam list): profitability concerns, scaling concerns, competitive concerns, a direct-to-consumer "ceiling," and a limited target market. A beloved community doesn't automatically make a sustainable, scalable business.

Brand Coolness, Rebranding & Relifting
Module II · Branding · Engineering a "cool" brand in the age of AI
▼

The big question

Almost every brand wants to be "cool," but very few achieve it. This session asks whether coolness can be deliberately engineered and managed rather than left to chance, and looks at it from both the brand's and a creative agency's point of view (including how AI is reshaping agencies).

Two views of any brand

A brand always exists in two places at once: Strategic Positioning = the desired image the company intends to project; Perceived Positioning = the image consumers actually hold. The gap between these two is the central thing a brand manager works to close.

How brands become cool

Coolness usually starts in a small niche, where a brand is seen as subcultural, rebellious, authentic, and original. Over time some of those niche brands get adopted by the masses and become perceived as popular and iconic instead. There's no single recipe, not every cool trait is needed, but increasing any of these qualities tends to make a brand feel cooler.

The two classic examples: Harley-Davidson became cool because an outlaw-biker subculture adopted it, lending it a rebellious, iconic image. BMW is cool for the opposite reason, it became a widely admired status symbol. Same outcome ("cool"), two completely different routes.

Rebranding/relifting is the practice of refreshing a brand's identity to regain relevance or coolness without throwing away its hard-won equity, the same balancing act as extending a campaign.

Building Brands with Social Responsibility
Module II · Branding · Case: "Green Gum", a sustainable brand born in the Mayan rainforest
▼

The situation

This case follows the genesis of a green, socially responsible brandan organic Mexican chewing gum (chicle) sourced from the Mayan rainforest, and uses it to teach how brands are built from scratch for the 21st-century consumer who cares about sustainability and ethics.

Branding is a point of view, not a to-do list

The course frames branding as a strategic point of view and a tool for lasting competitive advantage. Brands are described as conventional stories that circulate in society, written by four creators: companies, popular culture, customers, and influencers.

The 6-step branding process

  • 1. Understand the sources of a brand's meaning.
  • 2. Identify the cultural and customer sources that feed it (online reviews, mavens/connoisseurs, salespeople).
  • 3. Analyze competition and environment using Ohmae's 3C model, Company, Competition, Customerto spot branding opportunities.
  • 4. Design the brand strategy.
  • 5. Implement it.
  • 6. Evaluate it.

When is branding ethical?

The case argues branding is only a "benevolent" activity when four conditions hold: (1) companies and consumers have equal information about the product; (2) they have equivalent sophistication in understanding how branding works; (3) consumers aren't over-reliant on mental shortcuts (heuristics); and (4) the authors of the branding effort are revealed (no hidden manipulation). For a "green" brand this matters doubly, because false sustainability claims (greenwashing) violate these conditions.

Brand Crisis & Reputation Management
Module II · Branding · Case: United Airlines, Dr. Dao dragged off Flight 3411
▼

The situation

In 2017 United Airlines overbooked a flight and, when no one volunteered to leave, had aviation security forcibly drag a paying passenger, Dr. David Dao, off the plane. A bystander's video went viral within hours. The case is a textbook study of how a brand crisis explodes on social media and how (not) to respond.

How the crisis unfolded (the timeline)

  • April 9Chicago aviation security forcibly remove Dr. Dao from the plane; the video spreads globally.
  • April 10CEO Oscar Munoz issues a weak statement apologizing only for having to "re-accommodate" customers, widely seen as tone-deaf.
  • April 11United changes its policies.
  • April 12 to 13Public anger peaks; people demand Munoz resign; it emerges the airline never even contacted Dao's family.

The diagnosis: a bad initial response, poor and late communication, and overall mishandling. The case shows the impact through United's share-price drop and consumer-perception data.

What reputation is and why it's fragile

Reputation is how others view a person or company; it rests on trust and on consumers feeling good about buying from you. Social media can strengthen or destroy that reputation overnight, which is why communication strategy must be treated as a core part of overall marketing strategy.

The crisis-management playbook

  • Monitor constantly to catch early warning signs before they become crises.
  • Don't deny or dismisssaying "I don't know" or "we don't have that information yet" is better than inventing something that backfires.
  • Don't argue publicly; speak to those affected directly and personally.
  • Take community management seriously as an ongoing reputation-protection function.
  • Focus on substance over formaddress the real problem, not just the optics.

The case also references Tripp & Grégoire's research on online public complainingwhy customers complain online, how long they hold a grudge, how to respond after a complaint, and how to prevent complaints in the first place.

Brand Activism
Module II · Branding · Case: Nike & Colin Kaepernick, "Believe in something"
▼

The situation

In 2018 Nike made NFL quarterback Colin Kaepernicka polarizing figure for kneeling during the national anthem to protest racial injustice, the face of its "Just Do It" anniversary campaign ("Believe in something. Even if it means sacrificing everything"). It was a deliberate act of brand activism: taking a public stand on a divisive social issue. The case asks you to judge, using the data and exhibits, whether the gamble paid off, and which metrics actually answer that question (stock price, sales, purchase intent by demographic, emotional ad response, and ultimately Marketing ROI).

The three FIT dimensions

Activism works only when there is genuine fit. Evaluate three: Brand-Cause fit (does the cause align with what the brand stands for?), Cause-Company fit (does the company have credibility on this issue?), and Spokesperson-Cause fit (is the chosen person an authentic voice for the cause?). Kaepernick scored high on all three for Nike's athlete-empowerment identity.

Five types of activism

A brand can be socially active through: activism at work (internal/employee), political, environmental, economic, and legal activism.

Why brands do it, and the risk

Done well, activism makes consumers feel heard, integrates them into the brand's social purpose, motivates employees, and builds loyalty and social impact. The risk is alienating part of the audience, so the CMO and CFO want different evidence: the CMO looks at brand sentiment and engagement, the CFO at hard ROI and sales. Tools in the case include word-cloud sentiment analysis of social posts and analysis of effects on brand asset value.

Brand Architecture & Growth Strategies
Module II · Branding · Case: Grupo Britt, Costa Rican coffee goes global
▼

The situation

Grupo Britt is a Costa Rican company that started as a garage coffee roaster (1990) and grew into a travel-retail empire of "Britt Shops" in airports across Latin America. Its leader, Pablo Vargas, rose from intern to CEO. The company's culture prizes intrapreneurship ("anyone with an idea could bring it forward"), flat hierarchy, first-name basis, and environmental concern, and it built its own e-commerce technology early. The case decision: how should Britt grow from ~$133M (2015) toward ~$200M (2021)?

Brand architecture & the "multilocal" concept

Brand architecture is how a company organizes and relates its portfolio of brands and sub-brands. Britt's distinctive idea is being "multilocal"not a generic global brand, but one that feels authentically local in each country it operates (selling local coffee, crafts, and flavors as a sense-of-place souvenir), while running on shared systems behind the scenes.

The four growth alternatives

The heart of the case is choosing among four growth paths (an Ansoff-style menu), each demanding different capabilities:

  • A, Expansive: open Britt shops in new countries and airports. Needs relationships with airport authorities.
  • B, Intensive: add more shops within airports it's already in. Deeper penetration.
  • C, In-Depth: increase cross-selling, e.g., pushing products into supermarkets. Brand-stretch.
  • D, Disruptive: invent new travel-retail formats.

Britt's challenges are grouped as administrative (SKU control, diversification), operational (stiffer competition, limited airport space), and logistics/technology (complex logistics, tech maintenance). The lesson: growth isn't just opportunity, each path forces you to build new organizational capabilities.

Brand Authenticity & Global Markets
Module II · Branding · Case: Tequila Patrón, selling Mexican-ness back to Mexico
▼

The situation

Patrón launched in Los Angeles in 1989, when tequila was seen as a cheap shot-drink. Patrón's strategy was to create an entirely new "ultra-premium" category: teaching consumers to sip and taste tequila like a fine spirit, emphasizing raw-material quality and an artisanal production process. The dilemma in the case: how to position Patrón in Mexico itself, where it was viewed as an "American tequila" and its authenticity was therefore questioned.

How the product builds authenticity

Authenticity is grounded in craft. Patrón ages tequila in different barrels (new French oak, used American bourbon oak, Hungarian oak) and offers a line spanning unaged Silver, Reposado ("rested," ~3 to 5 months), and Añejo ("aged," ~12 to 14 months), plus the tahona-process Roca and triple-distilled Gran Patrón. Tequila aging categories follow Mexico's CRT norms (rested 2 to 12 months; aged 1 to 3 years; ultra-aged 3+ years). Blending ensures consistency and smoothness, the artisanal story is real, not just marketing.

The threats and the response

Two pressures: the explosive rise of mezcal (production up ~140% in 2012 to 13), which out-authenticities tequila in Mexican eyes, and the need to win the large 25 to 40, middle/upper-class segment without cheapening the brand. The response leans on communication through digital, trade, and influence marketing, plus education at points of contact (bars, restaurants, celebrities).

Guerrilla / grassroots marketing: Patrón notably says it has "never paid a penny" for celebrity endorsements, the brand spreads organically through word-of-mouth and unconventional, low-cost tactics. Grassroots marketing builds awareness from the ground up: it's slower and cheaper than mass advertising, but the loyalty it generates is genuine and long-lasting.

From the Wrap-Up: the authenticity equation

Authenticity is about closing the gap between expectation and reality"if your brand promises authenticity, you better deliver" (HBR 2014). People judge from experience, so the message must match the organization’s reality. The main tools to build it: consumer experience, public relations, points of sale, communication, and distribution channels.

Brand Love (Dr. Aaron Ahuvia)
Brand Love Workshop · How and why people fall in love with products, and the ethics of it
▼

Who & what

A four-session workshop by Dr. Aaron Ahuvia (University of Michigan-Dearborn), one of the world's leading researchers on the psychology of brand love. The framing is a three-act story: Act I, Boy finds love (people really do love products), Act II, Boy loses love (why almost all brands fail to be loved), Act III, Boy gets love back (the mechanism that makes brand love possible).

The core thesis: brand love is a "happy accident"

Love is a psychological system that evolved to make familiesto bond humans to other humans. It was never designed for products. So when we "love" a brand, we are accidentally firing human-bonding machinery at a commercial object. The practical implication: to build brand love, you must understand human love first.

Why so few brands are loved (Act II)

In Ahuvia's survey of 720 consumers, for every brand loved by more than 100 people there were over 250 brands that only a single person loved. Most companies wrongly picture a single spectrum from "hate" to "love" and assume that maximizing quality moves you up it. But quality and trust only get you a reliable product, "the difference between a keeper and a fling." They are necessary, not sufficient. You also need intrinsic rewards: the brand has to make you feel good in itself ("I love how I feel when I use this"), not merely be useful.

The mechanism: Personification (Act III)

The key insight is that products can only be truly loved once the mind treats them as people, not objects. Brand love becomes possible through three routes to personification:

  • 1. Anthropomorphismthe brand itself seems human. We talk to Siri, see a "face" on a watch, feel reluctant to "switch off" a talking toy even though we know it's plastic. Brands that take on a human-like personality become lovable.
  • 2. Connectingthe brand becomes part of another person you love (e.g., a perfume your mother wore), so your love for the person transfers to the object.
  • 3. Self-integrationthe brand becomes part of your own identity ("if this brand were a person, who would it be?", "my Mac would be a Victorian gentleman… and so would I"). Once something is part of the self, your brain processes it with the same self-serving bias you give yourself: you make excuses for its flaws instead of blaming it.

Segmentation for messaging

Ahuvia maps consumers on cultural capital × economic capital (e.g., "High Society," "BoBo / bourgeois-bohemian," "Middle Class," "Blue Collar"), each with distinct media, retailers, and lifestyle signals, so you can craft norms-based messaging that fits the group's identity.

The ethics (Session 13): "Don't be the devil incarnate." Capitalism, done right, is a positive-sum game you win by making others better off. The danger is temptation: older economic theory assumes people rationally weigh costs, benefits, risk, and timing (a personal "discount rate" for sooner-vs-later rewards), but real consumers can be tempted into self-destructive bargains. Ethical marketers move from an attention economy to an attachment economybuilding genuine, healthy love rather than exploiting impulses.

Entrepreneurial Branding
Entrepreneurship · Case: GümmyLife, from TikTok fame to a scalable brand
▼

The situation

GümmyLife is a young Mexican vitamin-gummy (nutraceutical) startup founded by three entrepreneurs. It exploded thanks to TikTok-native branding and a creator/affiliate sales model. In January 2026 the founders face a strategic decision: define a five-year plan amid a 2026 Mexican tax reform, intensifying social-commerce competition, and an increasingly sophisticated supplement market. The case is a playbook for turning a viral idea into a durable, scalable brand.

The business model

Conceptually GümmyLife is a blend of: TikTok-native branding + the creator economy (sales commissions) + performance marketing + AI-enabled personalization + AI-enabled social commerce + a direct-to-consumer (DTC) affiliate system, i.e., a platform/ecosystem strategy rather than a simple product company.

The strategic choice

To keep growing sustainably, which direction? (a) stay digital-first, (b) expand into physical retail, (c) diversify the product line, or (d) expand internationally. The case provides an Entrepreneurial Brand Scaling Framework and a Strategic Assessment Framework to evaluate each.

Three lessons that travel beyond gummies: (1) Attention ≠ loyaltygoing viral is not the same as building a base of repeat, committed customers. (2) Algorithmic visibility can accelerate growth but does not guarantee sustainabilityyou don't own the algorithm. (3) Growth changes the capabilities a company must buildthe skills that got you viral are not the skills that run a retail supply chain or an international operation.

Market context, five nutraceutical trends: personalization; demand for scientifically-backed functional ingredients; transparency as a competitive must; convenient product formats; and sustainability/ethical sourcing.
