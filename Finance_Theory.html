Part 1

Time value of money and rates

Present value and future value

Money today is worth more than the same amount later, because today's money can be invested and earn a return. Two moves cover most of the course.

  • Future value (FV): what an amount today grows into.
  • Present value (PV): what a future amount is worth in today's money.
Core formulas FV = PV × (1 + r)^n
PV = FV / (1 + r)^n
r = rate per period, n = number of periods

A higher rate or a longer wait makes a future cash flow worth less today. This single idea drives discounting, NPV, bond pricing, and valuation.

ExampleWorth 1,000 in 3 years, rate 6%. Today it is worth 1,000 / (1.06)^3 = 839.6. Run it forward and 839.6 grows back to 1,000.

Payments (annuities)

A payment stream is a series of equal cash flows at regular intervals: loan instalments, lease payments, bond coupons. The present value of an ordinary annuity (payments at the end of each period) is:

Annuity present value PV = PMT × [ 1 - (1 + r)^(-n) ] / r

You use this to find a loan payment, the value of a coupon stream, or the price of anything that pays a level amount for a fixed number of periods.

ExampleA 5-year stream of 200 a year, discounted at 8%, is worth 200 × [1 - 1.08^-5] / 0.08 = 200 × 3.993 = 798.5 today.

Recapitalization

Recapitalization means changing the mix of debt and equity in the capital structure, for example borrowing money to buy back shares. It does not change the operating business, but it changes financial risk, the cost of equity, and often the share price. This is the lever behind the American Greetings buyback discussion.

ExampleA firm borrows 100 and uses it to buy back 100 of stock. The assets and operating profit are unchanged, but debt rises by 100 and equity falls by 100, so the remaining shares now carry more financial risk.

Simple versus compound interest

Simple interest is earned only on the original principal. Compound interest earns interest on previously earned interest, so it grows faster over time.

The difference Simple: Interest = P × r × n
Compound: FV = P × (1 + r)^n

Over a single period the two are the same. Over many periods compound pulls ahead, and the gap is exactly the interest earned on interest.

Example1,000 at 10% for 2 years. Simple gives 1,000 + 1,000 × 0.10 × 2 = 1,200. Compound gives 1,000 × 1.10^2 = 1,210. The extra 10 is interest earned on the first year's interest.

Capitalization (compounding frequency) capitalización

Capitalization is the act of adding earned interest to the principal so that it also earns interest in the next period. How often you do this, the capitalization period, can be annual, semiannual, quarterly, monthly, daily, or continuous. It is the engine behind compound interest and the reason APR and EAR differ. The more frequently interest is capitalized, the faster the balance grows, because interest starts earning interest sooner.

Growth with m capitalizations per year FV = PV × (1 + r / m)^(m × n)
Continuous limit: FV = PV × e^(r × n), EAR = e^r - 1
r = nominal annual rate, m = capitalization periods per year, n = years
Example1,000 at 12% nominal for one year. Annual (m=1): 1,120.0. Monthly (m=12): 1,000 × (1 + 0.12/12)^12 = 1,126.8. Daily (m=365): about 1,127.5. Same nominal rate, more interest as capitalization gets more frequent.
Do not confuse the twoCapitalization here means compounding interest over time. Recapitalization, above, means changing a company's mix of debt and equity. Same root word, completely different idea. On the board, the capitalization note sits next to Payments because it governs how any interest or payment stream accumulates.

Discount rate

The discount rate is the rate you use to bring future cash flows back to today. It captures two things: the time value of money and the riskiness of the cash flows. More risk means a higher discount rate, which means a lower present value. The right rate depends on whose cash flows you are valuing: use the cost of equity for cash flows that belong to shareholders, and the weighted average cost of capital (WACC) for cash flows that belong to all investors.

Example1,000 due in one year. At a 5% rate it is worth 952.4 today; at 10% only 909.1. The riskier you judge the cash flow, the higher the rate, and the less it is worth now.

MARR minimum acceptable rate of return

MARR, also called the hurdle rate, is the lowest return a project must earn to be worth doing. A company sets it at or above its cost of capital. It plays two roles: it is the discount rate you put into the NPV calculation, and it is the bar you compare the IRR against. If a project cannot beat the MARR, you reject it.

ExampleA project returns 9% (its IRR) while the company's MARR is 12%. It falls short of the hurdle, so reject it, even though 9% is a positive return.

APR versus EAR (nominal versus effective)

These two rates describe the same loan or investment but answer different questions. They are also called the nominal rate and the effective rate, the words your course is likely to use. APR is the nominal rate, EAR is the effective rate.

  • APR, the nominal annual rate: the stated yearly rate, which ignores compounding within the year. APR = periodic rate × number of periods per year, so the periodic rate is just APR / m.
  • EAR, the effective annual rate: the true annual cost or return once you account for compounding inside the year.
Converting between them Nominal to effective: EAR = (1 + APR / m)^m - 1
Effective to nominal: APR = m × [ (1 + EAR)^(1/m) - 1 ]
m = capitalization (compounding) periods per year
Example24% APR compounded monthly. The periodic rate is 24% / 12 = 2% a month, and the effective annual rate is (1 + 0.24/12)^12 - 1 = 26.82%, noticeably above the quoted 24%.
Exam trapNominal and effective are equal only when compounding is annual (m = 1). With monthly or daily compounding, the effective rate is always higher than the nominal. When two products quote different compounding, compare them on the effective rate, never the nominal.
Part 2

Investment decision rules

Net present value (NPV)

NPV is the value a project adds, measured in today's money. You discount every future cash flow back to today and subtract the initial investment.

NPV NPV = - I₀ + Σ [ CFₕ / (1 + r)^t ] for t = 1 to n
Rule: accept if NPV > 0
ExampleInvest 50 now, receive 30 and 40 in years 1 and 2 at a 10% rate. Discounted inflows are 27.3 + 33.1 = 60.4, so NPV = -50 + 60.4 = +10.4. Positive, so accept.
Common slipThe most frequent error is forgetting the minus I₀ term. The discounted cash flows alone are gross present value. NPV is that total net of the upfront outlay. If you only sum the discounted inflows, you have overstated the result by the full investment.

Internal rate of return (IRR)

The IRR is the discount rate that makes a project's NPV exactly zero. It is the project's own built-in rate of return.

IRR Find r such that: - I₀ + Σ [ CFₕ / (1 + r)^t ] = 0
Rule: accept if IRR > MARR (the hurdle)

NPV and IRR usually agree, but IRR has two weak spots. With unconventional cash flows (the sign of the cash flow changes more than once) a project can have several IRRs. And when ranking mutually exclusive projects of different size, IRR can favor the smaller one even when the larger one adds more value. When NPV and IRR disagree, trust NPV.

ExampleInvest 100 now for a single 121 received in 2 years. The IRR solves 100 = 121 / (1+r)^2, so r = √(121/100) - 1 = 10%. If the MARR is 8%, accept; if it is 12%, reject.

Payback period and adjusted payback

The payback period is how long it takes to recover the initial investment from the project's cash flows. It is quick and intuitive, but it has two flaws: it ignores the time value of money, and it ignores everything that happens after the cutoff.

The adjusted (discounted) payback fixes the first flaw by discounting the cash flows before adding them up. It is more conservative, so the discounted payback is always longer than the simple payback. It still ignores cash flows after the cutoff, so it is a screening tool, not a final decision rule.

ExampleInvest 90, receive 50 a year. Simple payback is 90 / 50 = 1.8 years. Discounted at 10% the first two inflows are 45.5 and 41.3, only 86.8 by year 2, so the discounted payback is just over 2 years.

How these pieces connect

PV, the discount rate, NPV, IRR, and MARR are not separate tools. They are one chain of reasoning seen from different angles.

Future cash flows → Discount rate → Present value → NPV → IRR → MARR → Accept or reject

Start with future cash flows. Apply a discount rate, the price of time and risk, to bring each one back to today as a present value. Add up those present values and subtract the upfront investment, and you have the NPV, the value created in today's money.

Now turn the question around. Instead of fixing the rate and solving for value, fix the value at zero and solve for the rate. That break-even rate is the IRR, the project's own return. To judge it you need a bar, and that bar is the MARR, the hurdle rate, which is also the discount rate you fed into the NPV in the first place.

So the two decision rules are the same idea twice. NPV uses the MARR as the discount rate and asks, is value positive. IRR finds the rate that makes value zero and asks, does it beat the MARR. When the discount rate equals the MARR, accept if NPV > 0 is the same verdict as accept if IRR > MARR.

Boundary cases worth memorizing
  • When NPV = 0, the IRR equals the discount rate. If you discounted at the MARR and got NPV = 0, the project's IRR is exactly the MARR. That is the accept-reject line itself, the point where the project is just worth doing.
  • NPV > 0 says the same thing as IRR > discount rate, and NPV < 0 says the same as IRR < discount rate. For one normal project the two rules can never disagree; they only diverge when you rank competing projects.
  • If the discount rate were 0, NPV would just be the plain sum of the cash flows minus the investment. Every positive rate pulls NPV down from that ceiling, which is why a higher hurdle makes projects harder to pass.
  • A bond sits at par when coupon rate = YTM. Above that yield it falls to a discount, below it rises to a premium. Same logic as NPV: the price is just the cash flows discounted at the market rate.
  • In Gordon growth, if g = 0 the value is simply CF / r, a flat perpetuity. As g climbs toward r the denominator shrinks to nothing and the value runs to infinity, which is why g must stay below r and why terminal value is so sensitive.
  • When net debt = 0 (debt equals cash), enterprise value equals equity value. The bridge EV = Equity + Net debt collapses, so the firm path and the equity path land on the same number.
  • With no debt there is no interest and no borrowing, so FCFF = FCFE and WACC = Ke. The two DCF paths become one. Debt is the only reason they differ.
  • EAR = APR only when compounding is annual, and simple interest equals compound interest only over a single period. Add periods or within-year compounding and the two pull apart.
Part 3

Bonds

The building blocks

  • Face value (principal, nominal): the amount repaid at maturity, and the base on which the coupon is calculated. Often 100 or 1,000.
  • Coupon rate: the annual interest the bond pays, as a percent of face value. Coupon payment = coupon rate × face value. For a plain bond it is fixed.
  • Yield to maturity (YTM): the single discount rate that makes the present value of all the bond's future cash flows (the coupons plus the face value) equal to its current market price. It is the bond's IRR if you buy it now and hold it to maturity.

Face value, principal, and nominal value are three names for the same number, the amount the issuer promises to repay at maturity. Each name looks at it from a different angle. Face value is the literal sense, the amount written on the face of the bond. Principal is the borrowing sense, since a bond is a loan and the principal is the original sum that must be paid back, separate from interest. Nominal value is the stated sense, the amount in name, as opposed to what the bond actually trades for in the market.

The point that trips people up is that this number is fixed and does two jobs, while the market price floats. It is what you receive back at maturity no matter what the price did in between, and it is the base for the coupon: the coupon payment is the coupon rate times the face value, never times the price. So a 5% coupon on a 1,000 face bond pays 50 a year whether the bond trades at 920 or 1,080. The price is the present value of those coupons plus the face value discounted at the current yield, so the price moves while the face value stays anchored.

ExampleA 1,000 face bond with a 5% coupon pays 50 every year and returns 1,000 at maturity, even if its market price falls to 920. The 50 and the 1,000 come from the face value; only the 920 comes from the market.

The coupon rate is the interest rate the bond promises at the moment it is issued. It is set once, printed on the bond, and for a plain fixed-rate bond it never changes. It is always quoted on the face value, so the cash you collect each period is coupon rate times face value, a fixed amount. The thing to hold onto is that the coupon rate describes the cash the bond pays, not the return you earn. If you buy the bond for less or more than its face value, your actual return will not equal the coupon rate, even though the coupon cheque is the same every year.

ExampleA 6% coupon on a 1,000 face bond pays 60 a year, every year, for the life of the bond. That 60 does not change when the market price changes, because the coupon is figured on the 1,000 face, not on the price.

Yield to maturity (YTM) answers the question the coupon rate cannot: what return do I actually earn if I buy at today's price and hold the bond to the end. It is the one discount rate that makes the price equal the present value of every cash flow the bond will pay, the coupons plus the face value at maturity. That is exactly the IRR idea from projects, the rate that sets the present value of the cash flows equal to what you pay.

YTM differs from the coupon rate because you rarely pay exactly the face value. Your true return blends two things: the coupon income, and the gain or loss between the price you pay today and the face value you get back at maturity.

  • Buy below face (a discount): you also collect a gain at maturity, so YTM is above the coupon rate.
  • Buy above face (a premium): you take a loss back down to face at maturity, so YTM is below the coupon rate.
  • Buy at face (at par): no gain or loss, so YTM equals the coupon rate.

One more contrast worth fixing in mind: the coupon rate never moves, but the YTM moves every day, because the market price moves every day. When you hear that a bond "yields" a certain rate, that is the YTM, the live return at the current price, not the fixed coupon stamped on the bond.

ExampleA 5% coupon, 1,000 face bond bought for 920. You still collect 50 a year, and on top of that you gain 80 when the 1,000 is repaid at maturity. Coupon income plus that gain means your true yield, the YTM, is above 5%.
Bond price Price = Σ [ Coupon / (1 + y)^t ] + Face / (1 + y)^n
y = yield to maturity, n = periods to maturity
ExampleFace 1,000, coupon 5% (pays 50 a year). If the market YTM is also 5%, the price equals par, exactly 1,000. Coupon and yield match, so there is no premium or discount.

Price, yield, and the coupon

Bond prices and market interest rates move in opposite directions. When rates rise, the fixed coupons look less attractive, so the price falls, and the reverse when rates fall. The relationship between the coupon rate and the YTM tells you where the price sits:

ConditionBond trades atMeaning
Coupon rate > YTMPremiumPrice above face value
Coupon rate = YTMParPrice equals face value
Coupon rate < YTMDiscountPrice below face value
ExampleTake that 5% coupon bond. If market yields rise to 6%, the fixed 50 coupons now look low, so the price slips below 1,000. Rates up, price down.

A premium is the amount a bond's price sits above its face value, and a discount is the amount it sits below. The size is just price minus face: a 1,000 bond trading at 1,080 carries an 80 premium, one at 920 carries an 80 discount. The reason is always the same comparison. When the coupon beats the market yield, investors pay extra for the rich coupon, so the bond is priced at a premium; when the coupon lags the yield, they will only buy at a discount that makes up the difference.

One more idea the exam likes: the pull to par. Whatever premium or discount a bond carries, it shrinks as maturity approaches, because at maturity the bond pays exactly its face value. A premium bond drifts down toward face, a discount bond drifts up toward face, and both arrive at par on the maturity date, assuming the yield holds steady. So part of a premium bond's coupon is really getting your premium back, and a discount bond earns part of its return as that climb up to face.

ExampleA bond bought at a 920 discount on 1,000 face gives back 1,000 at maturity. The 80 climb to par is part of the return, which is why its YTM is above the coupon rate, the point from the YTM section.

Types of bonds

Zero-coupon bonds pay no coupons and are sold below face value, with the return coming entirely from the gain to maturity. Fixed-rate bonds pay level coupons. Floating-rate bonds reset their coupon to a market rate. Callable bonds let the issuer repay early. Convertible bonds can be exchanged for the issuer's shares, which links straight to the Spotify financing discussion. Bonds also split by issuer, mainly government versus corporate, with corporate bonds carrying more credit risk and so a higher yield.

ExampleA 1-year zero-coupon bond with face 1,000 priced to yield 5% sells today for 1,000 / 1.05 = 952.4. The whole 47.6 return is the gain to maturity, since there are no coupons.

Why hold a zero if it pays nothing along the way? The benefit is certainty. A coupon bond's quoted YTM quietly assumes you reinvest every coupon at that same rate, so if rates fall after you buy, you reinvest those coupons for less and your actual return slips below the quoted yield. That is reinvestment risk. A zero has no coupons to reinvest, so the yield you buy at is exactly the yield you earn if you hold to maturity. You know today precisely how much you will have on the maturity date.

That makes zeros the natural tool whenever you need a known amount on a known date. A pension fund or an insurer that owes a fixed sum in, say, 2040 buys a zero maturing then for exactly that amount, which is the idea of matching assets to liabilities. Traders also use long-dated zeros as a clean play on interest rates, since with all the value sitting at the end they are the most price-sensitive bond for their maturity, rising the most when rates fall. The most common zeros you will actually meet are short-term government instruments sold at a discount with no coupon: in Mexico that is CETES, and US Treasury bills work the same way.

Watch the trade-offsA zero pays no income along the way, its price swings the most if you have to sell early, and in many tax systems it generates taxable interest each year even though no cash arrives, which is why people often hold them in tax-sheltered accounts. How Mexico treats that is worth checking for your own situation.
Part 4

Valuation: projects and companies

The three families of valuation methods

  • DCF (discounted cash flow): forecast future cash flows and discount them to today. The most fundamental method, because it values the business from its own economics.
  • Multiples (relative valuation): value the company by comparison to similar companies, using ratios such as EV/EBITDA or P/E. Fast, market-based, but only as good as the comparables.
  • DDM (dividend discount model) and Gordon growth: value equity as the present value of expected dividends. The Gordon version assumes dividends grow at a constant rate g forever.
Gordon growth (constant-growth) value Value = CF₁ / (r - g) = CF₀ × (1 + g) / (r - g)
requires r > g; works for dividends, FCFE, or FCFF
ExampleNext year's dividend is 4, the cost of equity is 10%, and dividends grow at 3% forever. The share value is 4 / (0.10 - 0.03) = 57.1.

Which cash flows count: relevant, future, differential ReFuDi

For any decision, only three kinds of cash flow belong in the analysis.

  • Relevant: only cash flows that the decision actually changes. Sunk costs, money already spent, are ignored.
  • Future: only forward-looking cash flows matter. The past is gone.
  • Differential (incremental): the difference between doing the project and not doing it. Include opportunity costs and knock-on effects such as lost sales on existing products, and exclude fixed overhead that does not change either way.
ExampleYou already spent 5 last year on a market study. A new line needs 20 of equipment and will take 3 of sales from an existing product. Count the 20 and the 3 lost sales; ignore the 5, it is sunk.

FCF to firm and FCF to equity

These are the two parallel paths through a DCF. They differ in whose cash flow they measure and which rate discounts them.

ExampleFrom the table, FCFF is 39. Interest is 10 and tax is 30%, so after-tax interest is 7. With 6 of net new borrowing, FCFE = 39 - 7 + 6 = 38, which matches the FCFE column.
Why this mattersThe two methods should give the same equity value. They only agree when the interest charged in the income statement and the debt subtracted in the bridge describe the same debt. If the interest line implies a large debt balance but the bridge subtracts only a small net debt, the equity path carries a heavier interest load and comes out lower. That mismatch is exactly the kind of inconsistency to flag in a model.

So when do you use each one? They give the same answer in theory, but one is usually easier and cleaner for a given situation.

Capital budgeting decision

To decide on a project, build its differential free cash flow, discount it at the hurdle rate, and compute NPV. Accept any project with a positive NPV. When projects are mutually exclusive, choose the one with the highest NPV. The decision rests on incremental cash flow, never on accounting profit or allocated costs.

ExampleInvest 30 for differential cash flow of 12 a year for 4 years at a 10% hurdle. PV of the inflows is 12 × 3.170 = 38.0, so NPV = -30 + 38.0 = +8.0. Accept.

Valuing a company: historical, relative, and terminal value

  • Historical (book) approach: value equity at book value, total assets minus total liabilities. It is a floor and a sanity check, not market value.
  • Relative approach: apply peer multiples. EV/EBITDA is the workhorse (the American Greetings method). Asset-efficiency ratios feed the forecast: fixed-asset turnover = sales / fixed assets, and net working capital turnover = sales / net working capital. These tell you how much investment in plant and in working capital a given level of sales requires, which drives capex and ΔNWC in the cash flow.
  • Perpetuity (terminal value): the value of all cash flows beyond the explicit forecast, usually a Gordon-growth perpetuity discounted back to today. It often makes up most of the total value, so the growth rate and the discount rate dominate the answer.
ExampleRelative check: a peer trades at 8x EBITDA and the company earns 25 of EBITDA, implying an enterprise value near 8 × 25 = 200. Forecasting tie-in: if fixed-asset turnover is 2, then 100 of sales needs 100 / 2 = 50 of fixed assets, which sets the capex you must fund.

Market value, equity value, and firm value

These terms get mixed up constantly, so here is the precise picture with the formulas. They all answer the question "what is this worth," but they measure different claims and come from different places.

Market value of equity, the market capitalization. This is what the market currently says the shares are worth, updated every second the stock trades.

Market value formulas Market cap (market value of equity) = Share price × Number of shares
Rearranged: Share price = Equity value / Number of shares
Market value of the firm = Market value of equity + Market value of debt
ExampleA share price of 9.5 across 40 shares gives a market cap of 9.5 × 40 = 380. Turn it around and a 380 equity value across 40 shares is 380 / 40 = 9.5 per share.

Book value versus market value. The two are rarely equal. Book value comes from the balance sheet at historical cost; market value is what investors will actually pay, looking forward.

Book vs market equity Book value of equity = Total assets - Total liabilities
Market value of equity = Market cap (set by investors)
their ratio is the price-to-book multiple

Equity value is the value of the shareholders' claim. It can be a market figure (the market cap) or an intrinsic estimate you compute (a DCF on FCFE, or enterprise value minus net debt). Same claim, different ways to measure it.

Enterprise value, also called firm value, is the value of the whole operating business, to every provider of capital, debt and equity together. It does not depend on how the business is financed. The full bridge from equity to enterprise value is:

Enterprise value, full bridge EV = Market cap + Total debt + Preferred + Minority interest - Cash
Simplified: EV = Equity value + Net debt
Rearranged: Equity value = EV - Net debt
Net debt = Total debt - Cash
Why cash is subtractedCash is a non-operating asset. If you buy the whole company you also get its cash, which lowers the real cost of owning the operations. So enterprise value strips cash out to isolate the operating business, which is why the bridge uses net debt, not gross debt. Some courses, and quick exam questions, ignore cash and write firm value = equity + debt. That is the same idea with cash set to zero. Know both forms.
ExampleShare price 9.5 across 40 shares gives a market cap of 380. With total debt 150 and cash 30, net debt is 120, so enterprise value is 380 + 120 = 500. Working backward, equity value is 500 - 120 = 380, and per share 380 / 40 = 9.5.

Part 5

Applied cases

Harley Davidson: how growth and the hurdle rate move value

A DCF, and especially its terminal value, is extremely sensitive to two inputs: the long-run growth rate g and the discount rate, the MARR or WACC. Both sit in the denominator (r − g) of the perpetuity. A small rise in g or a small fall in the discount rate makes that denominator shrink and the value jump.

  • If your assumptions produce a value above the market price, the stock looks undervalued. That is the optimistic, bullish view.
  • If your value lands below the price, the stock looks overvalued. That is the cautious, bearish view.
ExampleA share worth 4 / (0.10 - 0.03) = 57.1 at 3% growth jumps to 4 / (0.10 - 0.04) = 66.7 at 4% growth, a 17% rise from one point of g. If the market price is 60, the first view is bearish and the second is bullish.
The lessonThe same model can say "buy" or "sell" depending on small changes to g and r. So a valuation is only as honest as its assumptions. Always state them, and test how the answer moves when you flex them. The conclusion is a range, not a single number.

Spotify: ways to raise equity capital

The Spotify case contrasts how a company can go public and finance itself.

  • Traditional IPO: the company issues new shares to the public through underwriters, who set the offer price and usually impose a lock-up period. It raises new cash but dilutes existing owners, the underwriting is expensive, and a large first-day price jump means the company sold too cheaply.
  • Direct listing: existing shares list directly on the exchange, with no underwriter setting a price and, in the classic form, no new shares issued. The market sets the price, existing holders can sell, fees are lower, and there is no dilution from new primary shares. The trade-off is that the company raises no new capital and gets no price support, so the opening can be volatile. This was Spotify's route.
  • Convertible debt: a bond that the holder can later convert into shares. The conversion option lets the issuer pay a lower interest rate than on straight debt, in exchange for potential future dilution if it converts. It is a flexible way to raise money around a listing.
  • Dilution: when new shares are issued, each existing share represents a smaller slice of ownership and earnings. Ownership percentage and earnings per share fall, unless the new capital is invested well enough to create value that offsets the larger share count.
ExampleA company with 100 shares issues 20 new ones in an IPO. Existing holders now own 100 / 120 = 83% of the company, so they are diluted by about 17%. A pure direct listing issues no new shares, so there is no such dilution.
Self-test

Multiple choice questions

Cover the answers, choose, then reveal. Each answer includes the reasoning, not just the letter.

Q1The present value of a fixed future cash flow falls when:

  1. the discount rate falls
  2. the discount rate rises
  3. the time to receipt shortens
  4. the cash flow increases
Answer: B.
PV = CF / (1 + r)^n. A higher r enlarges the denominator, so PV shrinks.

Q2Over several periods at the same positive rate, compound interest produces:

  1. the same amount as simple interest
  2. less than simple interest
  3. more than simple interest
  4. more only if the rate is below 10%
Answer: C.
Compounding earns interest on previously earned interest, so it outruns simple interest over multiple periods.

Q3Holding the nominal annual rate fixed, increasing the capitalization (compounding) frequency will:

  1. lower the future value
  2. raise both the future value and the EAR
  3. leave the future value unchanged
  4. matter only for simple interest
Answer: B.
More frequent capitalization means interest starts earning interest sooner, so FV rises and the effective annual rate climbs above the nominal rate. They coincide only with annual capitalization.

Q4A loan quotes 12% APR compounded monthly. Its effective annual rate is:

  1. exactly 12%
  2. less than 12%
  3. greater than 12%
  4. impossible to determine
Answer: C.
EAR = (1 + 0.12/12)^12 − 1 = 12.68%. Within-year compounding always pushes EAR above APR.

Q5The APR (nominal rate) and the EAR (effective rate) are equal only when:

  1. the rate is below 5%
  2. compounding is annual
  3. the loan is short term
  4. never
Answer: B.
With m = 1, (1 + APR)^1 − 1 = APR, so the two coincide.

Q6Under the NPV rule, you accept a project when:

  1. NPV > 0
  2. NPV < 0
  3. NPV equals the initial investment
  4. IRR equals zero
Answer: A.
A positive NPV means the project adds value in today's money.

Q7A frequent mistake in building the NPV formula is:

  1. discounting the cash flows
  2. forgetting to subtract the initial investment I₀
  3. using the hurdle rate as the discount rate
  4. summing the cash flows
Answer: B.
NPV is the discounted inflows net of the upfront outlay. Leave out −I₀ and you overstate value by the full investment.

Q8The IRR is best defined as:

  1. the project's accounting return
  2. the discount rate that makes NPV equal to zero
  3. the payback period
  4. the cost of equity
Answer: B.
IRR is the rate at which the project breaks even in present-value terms.

Q9On the IRR rule, accept the project when:

  1. IRR is below the MARR
  2. IRR equals zero
  3. IRR exceeds the MARR (the hurdle)
  4. IRR exceeds 100%
Answer: C.
If the project's own return clears the hurdle, it is worth doing.

Q10The MARR is:

  1. the maximum return allowed
  2. the minimum acceptable rate of return, the hurdle
  3. the market average return
  4. the risk-free rate only
Answer: B.
It is the bar a project must clear, usually set at or above the cost of capital.

Q11The main weakness of the simple payback period is that it:

  1. is hard to compute
  2. ignores the time value of money and cash flows after payback
  3. always rejects good projects
  4. requires the IRR first
Answer: B.
It treats near and far cash flows as equal and stops counting at the cutoff.

Q12Discounted (adjusted) payback differs from simple payback because it:

  1. ignores cash flows entirely
  2. uses discounted cash flows
  3. adds the terminal value
  4. uses book values
Answer: B.
It discounts each cash flow before accumulating, so the payback comes out longer.

Q13When a bond's coupon rate is below its YTM, the bond trades at:

  1. par
  2. a premium
  3. a discount
  4. face value
Answer: C.
A coupon that lags the market yield is worth less, so the price falls below face value.

Q14Bond prices and market interest rates:

  1. move together
  2. move inversely
  3. are unrelated
  4. move inversely only for zero-coupon bonds
Answer: B.
When rates rise, the fixed coupons are discounted harder and the price drops.

Q15Yield to maturity is essentially the bond's:

  1. coupon rate
  2. internal rate of return if held to maturity
  3. face value
  4. current yield
Answer: B.
YTM is the single rate that equates the price to the present value of all the bond's cash flows.

Q16As a bond approaches maturity, any premium or discount in its price tends to:

  1. grow larger
  2. shrink toward the face value
  3. stay exactly the same
  4. flip from premium to discount
Answer: B.
At maturity the bond repays exactly its face value, so the price is pulled to par. This is the pull to par.

Q17A zero-coupon bond held to maturity has no reinvestment risk because:

  1. it pays a higher coupon than other bonds
  2. it pays no coupons to reinvest, so the yield you buy at is the yield you earn
  3. its price never changes before maturity
  4. it is always issued by governments
Answer: B.
A coupon bond's quoted yield assumes you reinvest the coupons at that same rate. With no coupons, a zero locks in its yield to maturity from the start.

Q18To value the whole firm and reach enterprise value, you discount FCFF at:

  1. the cost of equity
  2. WACC
  3. the risk-free rate
  4. the coupon rate
Answer: B.
FCFF belongs to all investors, so it is discounted at the blended cost of capital.

Q19To reach equity value directly, you discount FCFE at:

  1. WACC
  2. the cost of equity (Ke)
  3. the pre-tax cost of debt
  4. the project hurdle rate
Answer: B.
FCFE is the shareholders' cash flow, so the cost of equity is the right rate.

Q20Starting from FCFF, you arrive at FCFE by:

  1. adding after-tax interest and subtracting net borrowing
  2. subtracting after-tax interest and adding net new borrowing
  3. discounting at WACC
  4. subtracting net debt
Answer: B.
FCFE = FCFF − after-tax interest + net new borrowing. FCFF is pre-financing, so you strip out the after-tax interest and add back the new borrowing to land on the shareholders' cash flow.

Q21The bridge from enterprise value to equity value is:

  1. Equity = EV + net debt
  2. Equity = EV − net debt
  3. EV = Equity − cash
  4. Equity = EV × (1 − tax)
Answer: B.
Subtract net debt (debt minus cash) from enterprise value to get what is left for shareholders.

Q22"Differential" cash flow in a project analysis means:

  1. all company cash flows
  2. only past cash flows
  3. the incremental change caused by the decision
  4. accounting profit
Answer: C.
Only the cash flows that differ between doing and not doing the project belong in the analysis.

Q23A sunk cost in capital budgeting should be:

  1. included as a cost
  2. excluded, because it is neither future nor differential
  3. added to the terminal value
  4. discounted at WACC
Answer: B.
It is already spent and does not change with the decision, so it fails the relevant, future, differential test.

Q24In a Gordon-growth terminal value, the value rises most when:

  1. the growth rate g falls
  2. the discount rate r rises
  3. the gap (r − g) narrows
  4. the cash flow falls
Answer: C.
Value = CF₁ / (r − g). A smaller denominator produces a larger value, which is why g and r dominate.

Q25If your DCF value per share sits above the market price, the stock looks:

  1. overvalued, a bearish signal
  2. undervalued, a bullish signal
  3. fairly valued
  4. illiquid
Answer: B.
Value above price means the market is paying less than the business is worth on your assumptions.

Q26Compared with a traditional IPO, a direct listing (Spotify) typically:

  1. raises more new capital
  2. issues no new primary shares and uses no underwriter to set the price
  3. guarantees a higher price
  4. prevents existing holders from selling
Answer: B.
In its classic form the existing shares simply list, the market sets the price, and there is no new-share dilution and no capital raised.

Q27Convertible debt is attractive to an issuer because it:

  1. never affects the share count
  2. carries a lower interest rate thanks to the conversion option, with possible future dilution
  3. must be repaid only in cash
  4. raises the cost of debt
Answer: B.
The embedded option to convert lets the issuer pay a lower coupon, at the cost of dilution if conversion happens.

Q28Dilution refers to:

  1. lowering the share price through a stock split
  2. each existing share owning a smaller slice after new shares are issued
  3. buying back shares
  4. paying a dividend
Answer: B.
More shares outstanding spreads ownership and earnings across a larger base.
Practice

Worked examples

Each one is solved step by step. Try it with the answer covered first, then check both the number and the method.

1. Present and future value, with compounding

Invest 10,000 at 8% per year for 5 years. Find the future value, then confirm the present value works back.

FV = 10,000 × (1.08)^5
= 10,000 × 1.46933
= 14,693.3

Check: PV = 14,693.3 / (1.08)^5 = 10,000 (back to the start)

2. Simple versus compound interest

Same 10,000 at 8% for 5 years. Compare the two methods.

Simple: 10,000 + (10,000 × 0.08 × 5) = 14,000.0
Compound: 10,000 × (1.08)^5 = 14,693.3

Difference = 693.3 this is the interest earned on interest

3. APR to EAR

A card charges 18% APR, compounded monthly. What is the true annual rate?

EAR = (1 + 0.18 / 12)^12 - 1
= (1.015)^12 - 1
= 1.1956 - 1
= 19.56%

The effective cost is almost 1.6 points above the quoted 18%, entirely from monthly compounding.

4. NPV of a project

Invest 100 today. Receive 40, 50, 60 at the end of years 1 to 3. Hurdle rate 10%.

PV year 1 = 40 / 1.10 = 36.36
PV year 2 = 50 / 1.21 = 41.32
PV year 3 = 60 / 1.331 = 45.08
Sum of discounted inflows = 122.76

NPV = -100 + 122.76 = +22.76 positive, so accept

Note the −100 at the front. Without it you would report 122.76 and wrongly conclude the project is far better than it is.

5. IRR by interpolation

Same project. Find the rate that drives NPV to zero.

At 10%: NPV = +22.76
At 25%: 40/1.25 + 50/1.5625 + 60/1.9531 - 100
= 32.00 + 32.00 + 30.72 - 100 = -5.28

The root sits between 10% and 25%. Interpolate:
IRR ≈ 10% + 22.76 / (22.76 + 5.28) × (25% - 10%)
≈ 10% + 0.812 × 15% = about 22.2%

Interpolation over a wide bracket only approximates, and it overstates here because the NPV curve bends. The exact IRR, the figure Excel's IRR function returns, is about 21.6%. Either way it clears the 10% hurdle comfortably, so accept. NPV and IRR agree.

6. Payback and discounted payback

Same project: invest 100, inflows 40, 50, 60. Discount at 10%.

Simple payback
After yr 1: recovered 40 (need 60 more)
After yr 2: recovered 90 (need 10 more)
In yr 3: 10 of 60 -> 0.17 of the year
Payback = 2.17 years

Discounted payback (using 36.36, 41.32, 45.08)
After yr 1: 36.36 (need 63.64 more)
After yr 2: 77.68 (need 22.32 more)
In yr 3: 22.32 of 45.08 -> 0.50 of the year
Discounted payback = 2.50 years

The discounted version is longer, because discounting shrinks the early inflows. That is the more conservative, time-value-respecting measure.

7. Bond price and the par relationship

A 5-year bond, face value 1,000, annual coupon 6% (so 60 a year), market YTM 8%.

Annuity factor = [1 - (1.08)^-5] / 0.08 = 3.9927
PV of coupons = 60 × 3.9927 = 239.56
PV of face = 1,000 / (1.08)^5 = 680.58

Price = 920.15

The price is below face value because the 6% coupon is lower than the 8% yield. Coupon under yield means the bond trades at a discount, as expected.

8. FCFF valuation and the enterprise-to-equity bridge

Firm cash flow: NOPAT 50, plus depreciation 20, minus capex 25, minus change in NWC 5. Grows at g = 2%, WACC = 10%, net debt = 120.

FCFF = 50 + 20 - 25 - 5 = 40

Enterprise value (Gordon)
EV = FCFF × (1 + g) / (WACC - g)
= 40 × 1.02 / (0.10 - 0.02)
= 40.8 / 0.08 = 510

Equity value = EV - net debt = 510 - 120 = 390

9. FCFE and the reconciliation to FCFF

Same firm. Interest expense 16, tax rate 30%, net new borrowing 6. Confirm the two cash-flow paths tie out.

From the firm side:
After-tax interest = 16 × (1 - 0.30) = 11.2
FCFE = FCFF - after-tax interest + net borrowing
= 40 - 11.2 + 6 = 34.8

From the equity side (bottom up):
Net income = NOPAT - after-tax interest = 50 - 11.2 = 38.8
FCFE = 38.8 + 20 - 25 - 5 + 6 = 34.8

The two routes agree because the interest and the borrowing are consistent. When a model's interest line implies a different debt level than the bridge subtracts, the equity values diverge. That is the inconsistency to hunt for.

10. Relative valuation with EV/EBITDA

Company EBITDA 80. Peer multiple EV/EBITDA = 7x. Net debt 120, shares 40.

Enterprise value = 7 × 80 = 560
Equity value = 560 - 120 = 440
Value per share = 440 / 40 = 11.0

If the stock trades at 9, the multiple approach says it is undervalued, a bullish read. Always sanity-check the multiple against the DCF, since they should land in a similar range.

11. Sensitivity: the Harley Davidson lesson

Take the FCFF = 40 firm from example 8 and flex one assumption at a time.

Base WACC 10%, g 2%: EV = 40 × 1.02 / 0.08 = 510
g up to 3% (WACC 10%): EV = 40 × 1.03 / 0.07 = 588.6 (+15%)
WACC down to 9% (g 2%): EV = 40 × 1.02 / 0.07 = 582.9 (+14%)

A single point of growth or a single point off the discount rate swings value by roughly 15%, because both inputs hit the (r − g) denominator. This is why the same model reads bullish or bearish depending on small assumption changes, and why you quote a range and name the assumption that moves it most.

New Heritage Doll CompanyCapital budgeting, choosing between two projects

The question

A doll maker can fund only one of two expansion projects, the Match My Doll Clothing Line expansion (MMDC) and Design Your Own Doll (DYOD). Build each project's free cash flow to the firm, add a terminal value, discount at the hurdle, and pick the one that creates more value.

The method

FCF to firm = NOPAT + Depreciation - Change in NWC - Capex
Terminal value (2020) = FCF₂₀₂₀ × (1 + g) / (DR - g)
tax 40%, g = 3.0%, discount rate DR = 8.4%

Working it out

ProjectOutlay at 2010Terminal valueNPV @ 8.4%IRR
Match My Doll (MMDC)(3,020)16,4017,20724.1%
Design Your Own (DYOD)(5,331)27,5959,45019.8%

Figures in thousands, from the professor's model. MMDC is his completed solve; DYOD is finished here with the same method, since his file left it at the projection stage.

The read

DYOD has the higher NPV, 9,450 against 7,207, so on pure value creation it wins. But it needs far more upfront, 5,331 against 3,020, earns a lower IRR, 19.8% against 24.1%, and is the newer, less proven concept. So NPV points to DYOD while IRR, capital efficiency, and risk point to MMDC. That tension, NPV versus IRR for competing projects under capital rationing, is the heart of the case. Most of each value sits in the terminal value, so g and the discount rate dominate the answer.

Navios Mercantes do BrasilA replacement decision: retrofit the old ship or buy a new one

The question

A Brazilian shipper must meet new emissions rules. It can keep its ship, the Constituicao, and install scrubbers, or replace it with a new LNG-powered ship, the Cynthia. The revenue is the same either way, so the two are compared as cost streams: discount each at the hurdle and choose the lower-cost present value.

After-tax salvage in the replacement option

Selling the old ship and scrubber at a loss creates a tax shield:
Old ship: MV 10.1, BV 16.0 -> loss 5.9 -> shield 2.07 -> after-tax 12.17
Old scrubber: MV 3.0, BV 6.4 -> loss 3.4 -> shield 1.19 -> after-tax 4.19
Cynthia outlay at t0 = -23.0 (ship) - 6.3 (inventory) + 12.17 + 4.19 = -12.9

Comparing the two

AlternativeOutlay at t0OPEX per yearNPV of costs @ 10%
Keep ship, install scrubbers(4.1)9.5(27.7)
Replace with Cynthia (LNG)(12.9)7.0(28.5)

Figures in millions, from the professor's model. Tax 35%, MARR 10%, seven-year horizon.

The read

The LNG ship runs cheaper, OPEX of 7 against 9.5, but it carries a much larger net investment, a mid-life motor overhaul, and salvage at the end. The question

A motorcycle dealer in Torreon plans to open a Monterrey branch with a new partner, Joaquin, who would take a 33.33% stake. Value the combined business to see what the equity, and Joaquin's share, is really worth.

Working it out

Tottenham HotspurFCFF, FCFE, and a reconciliation that does not tie

The question

Value the football club two ways, the firm path and the equity path, and reconcile them against the market. When they do not line up, find out why.

Working it out

FCFF DCF: enterprise value ≈ 135, less net debt 16.8 -> equity ≈ 118
FCFE DCF (at Ke): equity ≈ 85
Market capitalization: 128

The read

The three do not agree, and the reason is a consistency gap. The interest charged in the income statement implies gross debt of roughly 40 rising toward 110, but the bridge subtracts only 16.8 of net debt. So the equity path pays interest on a much larger balance every year while the firm path nets out only a small debt, which is exactly why the FCFE value comes in low. The two paths reconcile only once the interest line and the debt figure describe the same debt.

Harley DavidsonDCF, and how growth and the hurdle rate move value

The question

Value the business with a discounted cash flow, then test how the answer moves under bullish and bearish assumptions. Is the stock under or overvalued? The lesson is sensitivity: the terminal value runs through the (r − g) denominator, so small changes swing the result.

Working it out

Start from a next-year firm cash flow of 600, net debt of 1,000, and 250 shares. Value the firm with the Gordon perpetuity, bridge to equity, divide by shares, then flex g and the WACC.

Base WACC 9.0%, g 3.0%: EV = 600 / (0.090 - 0.030) = 10,000
Equity = 10,000 - 1,000 = 9,000 -> per share 36.0
Bear WACC 10.0%, g 2.0%: EV = 600 / 0.080 = 7,500
Equity = 6,500 -> per share 26.0
Bull WACC 8.5%, g 4.0%: EV = 600 / 0.045 = 13,333
Equity = 12,333 -> per share 49.3
ScenarioValue per shareVersus a 35 price
Bearish26.0Overvalued, a sell view
Base36.0About fair
Bullish49.3Undervalued, a buy view

The same model says sell, hold, or buy depending on one point of growth and half a point of discount rate. That is the whole point of the case: a valuation is a range, not a single number, so you state your assumptions and show the sensitivity rather than defend one figure.

SpotifyDirect listing versus a traditional IPO

The question

How should the company go public? Compare a traditional IPO, which issues new shares through underwriters, against a direct listing, which lists existing shares with no new issuance and lets the market set the price.

Working it out

Take a company with 180 existing shares. The traditional IPO raises cash by issuing 20 new shares; the direct listing issues none.

Traditional IPO
New shares issued = 20
Total shares after = 180 + 20 = 200
Existing holders now own = 180 / 200 = 90%
Dilution = 20 / 200 = 10%
Plus underwriting fee on proceeds and any first-day price pop

Direct listing
New shares issued = 0
Total shares after = 180
Existing holders now own = 100%
Dilution = none; no capital raised; market sets the price

The read

Spotify chose the direct listing. It did not need new cash, so it avoided the 10% dilution and the underwriting fee, and it let the market set the price instead of an underwriter. The trade-off is that it raised no new capital and got no price support, so the opening can be more volatile.
