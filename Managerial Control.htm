IPADE · Organizational Control · Exam Revision

Every control tool is judged by one thing: its effect on behavior.

Management control sits where Strategy, the Person, and the Management Control System meet. The objective is always goal congruence: aligning individual behavior with what the organization wants. This guide is built to apply, not memorize, because the exam is a live case.

Exam format: a case study on Friday, covering all 14 cases. You diagnose the situation, pick the tool, and defend the design. A past exam used the Henkel case, worked through near the end of this guide.
FOUNDATIONS

The backbone of every answer

Two questions crack any case. They appear verbatim in the Empire Glass and Thorsten decks. Start every essay here, then choose the tool that fixes the specific problem in front of you. Control itself is forward-looking: it is about moderating future performance and incentivizing the correct behavior.

1
Diagnostic question one

What does the company want?

The strategy. What it is competing on, what would kill it if it failed.

2
Diagnostic question two

How does it intend to achieve it?

The structure plus the control system: budgets, responsibility centers, transfer pricing, scorecards, incentives.

The crucial aspect of any control system is its effect on behaviour. Lawler & Rhode · the decisive test repeated across the course
The single highest-value line

There are no a-priori absolute rules. The design must fit the organization's strategy, structure, and Critical Performance Variables, and is judged by its effect on behavior. Use this to open or close any essay (Empire Glass, echoed in Birch Paper).

DECISION TOOL

Which framework fits the problem?

Tap the problem the case puts in front of you. The exam rewards picking the right tool for the right reason, not listing all of them.

Budgets

Tool · Sessions 1 & 2
Use when the problem is planning, coordinating, and motivating around financial targets and resource allocation.
  • Always flag the three-role conflict: one number cannot be optimal for planning, motivation, and evaluation at once (Hala Madrid).
  • Show the build-and-stress-test mechanics: Profit Wheel → Cash Wheel → ROI Wheel (MVS).
  • Weighting trap: over-relying on one number for three incompatible jobs.
Anchor cases: Real Madrid (the conflict), MVS (the mechanics).

Financial Responsibility Centers

Tool · Session 4
Use when the problem is who is accountable for what. You change behavior by changing what you measure.
  • Choose cost, profit, or investment centers to direct each manager to the variables they actually control.
  • Weight to the firm's degree of decentralization and its Critical Performance Variables.
  • A scheme that looks wrong at first glance can be right for this organization's context.
Anchor case: Empire Glass. Punchline: there are no a-priori absolute rules.

Transfer Pricing

Tool · Sessions 5 & 6
A sub-problem that appears once you create trading profit centers. It is inseparable from make-vs-buy and capital budgeting.
  • Separate the company-wide decision (use relevant or incremental cost) from the transfer-pricing decision (a distribution-of-profit question between divisions).
  • High interdependence and vertical integration: mandate internal sourcing at cost or standard cost.
  • High diversification and independence: use market-based prices or little internal transfer pricing at all (Eccles).
  • Always reconcile divisional optima against the company-wide optimum.
Anchor cases: A.B. Thorsten (make-vs-buy flips on the price), Birch Paper (methods and pathologies).

Interactive Control

Tool · Session 3
Use when strategy depends on innovation and adaptation under uncertainty, and rigid targets would do damage.
  • Reduce reliance on formula-based target achievement; use the budget to drive dialogue and learning.
  • Reward effort and process; use subjective evaluation by superiors.
  • Shields managers from uncertainty they do not control and protects honest information-sharing.
Anchor case: Codman & Shurtleff / J&J. The counterpoint to rigid-budget pathologies.

Balanced Scorecard

Tool · Session 10
Use when you must translate strategy into balanced, causally-linked, leading-plus-lagging measures and you fear pure financial control is too short-term.
  • Four linked perspectives, connected by an explicit cause-and-effect strategy map.
  • Content is strategy-specific: the perspectives are not generic checklists.
  • Balance means not over-indexing on the Financial perspective at the expense of its drivers.
Anchor case: Delta / Signal Corp. simulation (Kaplan & Norton).
THE CASES

Eight cases, one logic

Each card set follows the same structure so you can rehearse the exam move: situation, the concept, the theory, how the case applies it, and the exam-ready lesson.

SESSION 01
Budgets

Hala Madrid: Managing Real Madrid

The three conflicting roles of a single budget number.

The situation

Real Madrid in the Galacticos era. Management control framed as the link between Strategy, the MCS tools, and the Person, with goal congruence as the objective.

The theory

For planning you want the realistic outcome; for motivation a difficult-but-attainable fixed standard; for evaluation an ex-post figure adjusted for controllable items. One number cannot be optimal for all three.

How the case applies it

It dramatizes the behavioral conflict as incentives to optimism versus incentives to prudence. The budget gets biased because the people setting it have stakes in pulling it up or down.

Exam lesson

Explain why budgets cause dysfunctional behavior. The fix: separate the roles. A planning forecast, distinct from the motivational target, distinct from the evaluation baseline.

The three objectives of budgeting

1. Translate the business strategy into a detailed plan that generates value. 2. Evaluate whether the company has enough resources to implement the desired strategy. 3. Link the economic objectives with the strategic performance indicators.

SESSION 02
Budgets

MVS: Designing a New Business Model

How to actually build and stress-test a budget for a go / no-go decision.

The situation

A "should we launch?" decision built on Simons' Profit Wheel, Cash Wheel, ROI Wheel.

The theory

Two philosophies: traditional incremental (extrapolate last year) versus zero-based budgeting (build from scratch off assumptions, not copy-paste from last year). Profit Wheel: volume → operating expenses → EBITDA → evaluate investments (working capital plus fixed assets) → test via sensitivity.

How the case applies it

You construct the launch budget, classify every cost, compute an often-negative early EBITDA, then attack: is volume realistic, will fixed costs stay fixed, is zero-financing valid?

Exam lesson

Use for the mechanics and the discipline of stress-testing. A budget's credibility lives in its assumptions, not its bottom line.

Which wheel comes first (the professor's framing)

The build sequence is Profit then Cash then ROI, but think about entry from the investor's side. You need investment and cash before profit and growth can follow, so reading the wheels Cash to Profit to Growth (or ROI first, then profit, then cash) keeps the capital question in front. The order you choose signals whose question you are answering first.

SESSION 03
Budgeting process

Codman & Shurtleff / Johnson & Johnson

Diagnostic versus interactive control. Can innovation be controlled?

The situation

J&J's planning and control system on an innovative subsidiary: the 5/10-year plan, annual profit plan, the "Burke letter," repeated debate-and-adjust cycles.

The theory

Simons' split: diagnostic (targets, variances, exception) versus interactive (senior managers personally and regularly engage to force attention and learning).

How the case applies it

Rewards on effort and process rather than purely on hitting targets, using subjective evaluation. Repeated review cycles generate organizational learning.

Exam lesson

When strategy depends on innovation under uncertainty: deliberately reduce formula-based achievement, use the budget interactively, reward effort.

SESSION 04
Responsibility centers

Empire Glass

Financial responsibility centers. You change behavior by changing what you measure.

The situation

Responsibility-accounting case mapping budgeting roles: Product Division VP, market research, district sales managers, plant managers, controller.

The theory

Assign responsibility via cost, profit, or investment centers to orient attention to controllable variables. Structure must fit strategy and the firm's Critical Performance Variables (the activities whose failure threatens survival).

How the case applies it

A scheme that looks "wrong" at first glance is actually right for this organization's context: it aligns the otherwise-conflicting interests of production and sales.

Exam lesson

Decide who is accountable for what. The most quotable line in the course: there are no a-priori absolute rules.

Critical performance variables, mapped to owners

In Glass Co the CPVs sit where they are controllable: Quality, Service, and Cost are the Plant's responsibility; Price belongs to the Market (sales). Structure design follows from the CPVs, not the other way around.

Why treat the plant as a profit center, not a cost center

A pure cost center tempts managers to cut corners. The profit-center framing keeps the plant focused on production, quality, and service, and removes the sales-versus-plant conflict, so both hit their goals without fighting. The catch (and the future risk): it works while the market environment holds; a shift in that environment is where it can break.

The fourth type: don't forget the revenue center

Beyond cost, profit, and investment centers there is the revenue center, accountable for sales mix and volume. In Empire Glass this is the sales side. Four types in total, each driving different behavior based on what it is held accountable for.

SESSION 05
Transfer pricing

A.B. Thorsten

Internal markets meeting make-vs-buy and capital-investment decisions.

The situation

Belgian parent versus Swedish subsidiary: build a local plant for XL-25, or keep importing from Belgium?

The theory

Once units are profit centers trading internally, the transfer price determines which option looks profitable and for whom (variable cost, total cost, or market price plus transport and import tax).

How the case applies it

Two linked questions: should the plant be built, and at what price should Belgium sell. The answer to the first depends entirely on the second.

Exam lesson

Separate the company-wide decision (relevant or incremental cost) from the transfer-pricing decision (distribution of profit between divisions).

SESSION 06
Transfer pricing

Birch Paper Company

Methods, pathologies, and the strategy / structure resolution.

The situation

Three internal divisions trade and must set the transfer price for boxes; an internal bid ends up uncompetitive against outside suppliers.

The theory

Methods: market-based vs cost-based (variable, full, standard, cost-plus, ABC), negotiated, or dual pricing. Pathologies: price manipulation to hit own goals, disputes, captive-supplier distortion, externally uncompetitive final product.

How the case applies it

The numbers show divisional-profit logic can be wrong for the company. The expensive-looking internal bid may be cheapest in incremental terms.

Exam lesson

Choose the method by interdependence; always reconcile divisional optima with the company-wide optimum.

Transfer pricing as a behavior lever

The price is a tool to align behavior with strategy, and the policy you pick sets the relationship. Raw material (variable) cost only creates a cooperative, "good brother" dynamic between divisions; market price plus a margin introduces tension and arm's-length bargaining. It must align with the financial responsibility centers, and it applies to internal services too, not just physical goods (for example, HR "selling" services to Operations).

SESSION 10
Balanced scorecard

Delta / Signal Corp. Simulation

Translating strategy into balanced, causally-linked measures (Kaplan & Norton).

The situation

A struggling auto-parts maker. You pick a strategy and manage through a scorecard over multiple periods, funding initiatives and watching metrics respond.

The theory

Financial measures are lagging. The BSC translates strategy across four linked perspectives connected by explicit cause-and-effect: Learning & Growth → Internal Process → Customer → Financial.

How the case applies it

The defining move is the strategy choice up front: a segment (Luxury vs Economy) and a generic strategy. Right objectives differ entirely by strategy. It runs as an interactive control system.

Exam lesson

Use when you must translate strategy into leading-plus-lagging measures. "Balance" means not over-indexing on Financial at the expense of its drivers. Same "fit to strategy" principle, applied to measurement.

Lag indicator

Measures the result of past actions: it tells you what already happened. An output that confirms whether an objective was achieved, but by the time it is visible there is little you can do to influence it.

Lead indicator

Measures the drivers of future performance. Tells you what is happening now that will likely determine tomorrow's outcomes. Handled well, it predicts movement in the lag indicator it is linked to, giving management early warning and room to course-correct.

Lead predicts lag, mapped to the four perspectives

Sales calls per week predict revenue (Financial). On-time delivery rate predicts customer retention (Customer). Hours of employee training per quarter predict innovation capacity (Learning and Growth). Preventive maintenance completion rate predicts equipment downtime (Internal Process). Each lead sits one perspective below the lag it drives.

BSC APPLIED
Balanced scorecard

Mobil: a scorecard built on a sharpened strategy

The worked example of the Delta / Signal logic. Mobil reversed losses by first redefining the strategy, then cascading it into a scorecard tied to compensation.

The shift: previous → new

All customers are good → focus on three segments. Volume and margin focus (the dollars) → customer service and internal processes. Short-term KPI focus → a longer-term integrated strategy.

The strategy statement, in three parts

Objective: an ambitious goal with a defined date, to create urgency for change. Competitive advantage: what the company does differently or better, and why rivals cannot copy it. Reach / positioning: the segment, product breadth, technologies, and geography where it competes.

Mobil, stated

Objective: reverse operating losses and move from worst to best performer in the industry on profitability. Advantage: fast, friendly, safe service; convenience stores redesigned from impulse-buy to destination shops; a competitive cost structure through value-chain integration; a scorecard cascaded across all business units and linked to variable pay. Reach: deliberately abandon the mass market, walk away from price shoppers, concentrate on the 18 states where they held a 12 percent share rather than spreading thin.

The cascade (your sequence)

After defining the strategy: set strategic objectives, reflect each one in a measurement with a goal, then define the actions, and read the results. This is the same objective → metric → initiative chain as Delta / Signal, run end to end on a real firm.

NONPROFIT CONTROL
Value / Support / Capacity

Kaboom: control without a profit line

The nonprofit case. With no profit to optimize, success runs on a different model: an organization must deliver something of value through the willing support of many people who together hold enough capacity in resources and skills. All three are required at once.

The loop, mapped to the scorecard

Input → Process → Output. Input is Learning and Growth; Process is Internal processes; Output is Customer and Financial. The same I, P, O loop from the backbone, read through the four scorecard perspectives.

Strategy modes

KaBOOM! runs three modes: Lead (run the build itself), Seed (give communities the tools and grants to build their own), and Rally (advocacy and movement-building). Different modes lean on different mixes of value, support, and capacity.

Exam lesson

When the case is a nonprofit, do not look for a profit center. Judge control by whether the organization sustains value, support, and capacity together, and map its objectives through the input-process-output loop into scorecard perspectives just as a for-profit would.

FOUNDATIONS
Simons

The four levers of control

The bigger frame that diagnostic and interactive control sit inside. Control tools exist to make people willing to behave as the strategy needs, while avoiding unpleasant, relevant surprises (the hundred-million fraud charge you never saw coming). The only thing a company really controls is people; they perform the activities that drive results.

THE ACTIVATION LAYER
Incentives

Incentives: where measurement becomes behavior

No standalone case PDF. Incentives are woven through every case. The central risk: you get exactly what you reward.

What an incentive system is for

A way to modify behavior and a tool to help reach goals. It measures people's performance, attracts the kind of people you want, and communicates what actually matters to the company.

The scorecard-plus-incentives test

One objective of a balanced scorecard with an incentive system attached: how do we get the company's priorities onto people's agendas on a Monday morning? If the measures and rewards do not change what someone does first thing Monday, the system is not working.

DRY RUN
Likely exam case

Henkel: applying the whole toolkit

A past exam used this case, and it is written by Robert Simons, the author of most of the frameworks above, so it is the ideal rehearsal. Walk it through the exam skeleton: what does the company want, how does it intend to get there, and does the control design produce goal-congruent behavior.

The situation

Kasper Rorsted becomes CEO of Henkel (German maker of laundry and home care, beauty care, and adhesives) in 2008. The culture is comfortable and stable; many managers have never had negative feedback. He sets out to replace "good enough" with a winning culture focused on outperforming rivals.

What he changed (the control system)

In November 2008 he announced ambitious financial targets for 2012, and reaffirmed them straight through the financial crisis. He cut the ten old values to five (customers first), and built a simplified performance system rating managers on a four-point scale for performance and potential, with forced ranking (set percentages of top, strong, moderate, low). Ratings drove bonuses; top managers' bonuses would double if the 2012 targets were hit.

Map it to the frameworks

Budgets, the motivation role: the 2012 targets are deliberately aspirational and fixed, used to motivate, not to forecast. Refusing to revise them through the crisis is the textbook separation of the motivation role from the planning role (Hala Madrid). Levers of control: the five values are a belief system; the targets and the four-point ratings are diagnostic control. Incentives: forced ranking plus bonus doubling is "you get exactly what you reward," aimed at killing complacency.

The tension to argue both sides of

Does the design produce goal-congruent behavior, or dysfunction? For: it broke complacency, sharpened accountability, and hit the targets. Against: forced ranking can drive gaming, short-termism, and loss of cooperation (the Lawler and Rhode warning), and rigid targets can become dangerous if the environment shifts. A strong answer judges the system by its effect on behavior and ties the verdict to Henkel's strategy and critical performance variables.

If your course used a different Henkel variant, the facts above are from Simons and Kindred's "Building a Winning Culture." Check any figures against your own case packet before the exam.

Vignette · the optimistic forecast
1. A division president sets next year's budget. He is up for reelection by the board, wants to justify three expensive hires, and the same number will be used to plan cash, to motivate his team, and to evaluate him in twelve months. The number looks aggressive. What is the core control failure?

Vignette · build or import
5. A Swedish subsidiary's NPV case for building a local plant looks strong. HQ insists it is cheaper to make centrally and export. Both used different transfer prices. What must you do first?

Vignette · the scorecard trap
7. A firm pursuing a Product Innovation strategy builds a scorecard but loads it almost entirely with revenue and margin metrics. What is the design flaw?
This is Delta / Signal. Financial measures are lagging; balance means funding the drivers. The perspectives are not generic checklists: a Product Innovation strategy weights R&D throughput and engineering talent, linked by cause-and-effect to financial results.
Vignette · the new CEO
8. A new CEO inherits a comfortable firm where managers rarely get negative feedback. He sets ambitious multi-year targets, refuses to lower them when a downturn hits, cuts the values list down, and ties bonuses to a forced ranking. An analyst calls the fixed targets unrealistic. Best read?
This is Henkel. Aspirational fixed targets are the motivation role separated from planning (Hala Madrid); the values are a belief system and the ratings are diagnostic control (Simons' levers); forced ranking plus bonus doubling is pure incentive design.
