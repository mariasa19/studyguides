Define

Re-envisioning Social Responsibility: Limits & Scope

▾
Key Frameworks
Carroll's CSR PyramidShareholder vs. Stakeholder viewInstrumental vs. Normative CSRKarnani's Trade-off / Win-win Zone
Core Concepts
What CSR means and where its boundaries lie. Friedman's profit-maximization view vs. Freeman's stakeholder theory. Carroll's pyramid: economic, legal, ethical, philanthropic. License to operate; greenwashing risk.
In Depth
The foundational debate is normative. Friedman (1970) argued the firm's only social responsibility is to increase profits within legal/ethical custom, because managers are agents of shareholders. Freeman's stakeholder theory (1984) counters that the firm is a nexus of relationships with employees, customers, suppliers, communities and financiers, all of whom have legitimate claims. Carroll's pyramid integrates both by ordering responsibilities: economic and legal are required, ethical is expected, philanthropic is desired. The 'limits and scope' framing pushes you to ask where corporate responsibility legitimately ends and where it becomes overreach or mere window-dressing.
Further Considerations
  • Distinguish the four CSR motivations: legal compliance, risk management, reputation, and genuine ethical conviction.
  • 'License to operate' = the implicit social permission a firm needs from communities and regulators.
  • The instrumental trap: if CSR is justified only when it pays, what happens when doing right costs money?
  • Visser's critique: classic CSR has failed to move the needle at scale, prompting 'CSR 2.0'.
The Tension
⚡ Is CSR a genuine reorientation of corporate purpose, or a peripheral reputational add-on?

Define

Responsibility & Purpose in Fragile Contexts

▾
Key Frameworks
Base of the Pyramid (Prahalad)Institutional Voids (Khanna & Palepu)Corporate Purpose
Core Concepts
Applying responsibility in weak-institution, emerging-market settings. Firms substitute for missing institutions (courts, credit, infrastructure), raising costs but creating value opportunities. Purpose as a stabilizing anchor.
In Depth
Prahalad's Base of the Pyramid reframed ~4 billion low-income people not as charity recipients but as an underserved market whose needs drive frugal innovation. But operating there means confronting institutional voids (Khanna & Palepu): missing or unreliable courts, credit bureaus, logistics and labor intermediaries. Firms either build these capabilities themselves (costly, but a moat) or partner. 'Fragility' adds conflict, corruption and weak enforcement, raising ethical hazards: a firm can extract value while externalizing harm precisely because no one is watching. Purpose becomes the internal compass when external rules cannot be trusted.
Further Considerations
  • Frugal / 'jugaad' innovation: doing more with less for resource-constrained markets.
  • The 'fortune' critique (Karnani): BoP can exploit rather than empower the poor.
  • Do-no-harm principle and conflict-sensitive business practice in fragile states.
  • Purpose as governance: a clear mission reduces the discretion that corruption exploits.
The Tension
⚡ How do you define and act on 'purpose' when the surrounding system can't be relied on?

Strategize

Business Model for Sustainability: Defining Strategy

▾
Key Frameworks
Creating Shared Value (Porter & Kramer)Sustainable Business Model CanvasTriple Bottom Line
Core Concepts
From philosophy to strategy. CSV's three levers: reconceive products/markets, redefine value-chain productivity, build clusters. Sustainability embedded in the model vs. bolted on.
In Depth
Creating Shared Value (Porter & Kramer 2011) is the strategic pivot: competitiveness and community health are interdependent. Its three levers are (1) reconceiving products and markets to meet social needs profitably, (2) redefining productivity in the value chain by addressing resource use, supplier health and logistics, and (3) enabling local cluster development. The Sustainable Business Model Canvas extends Osterwalder's nine blocks with environmental and social value propositions and captures value destroyed and value missed. The test is integration: is sustainability a separate department, or the logic of the model itself?
Further Considerations
  • CSV critiques (Crane et al.): it ignores tensions/trade-offs and assumes win-wins always exist.
  • Triple bottom line (Elkington), note its author later 'recalled' it for being reduced to accounting.
  • Differentiate compliance-driven, eco-efficiency, and transformative strategy postures.
  • Map sustainability to genuine competitive advantage: cost, differentiation, or risk reduction.
The Tension
⚡ Defensive/compliance strategy vs. strategic/transformative, trade-offs vs. genuine win-wins.

Strategize

Internal or External Capacities? The Non-Profit Sector as an Ally

▾
Key Frameworks
Resource-Based ViewTransaction-Cost EconomicsAustin's Collaboration Continuum
Core Concepts
Build-vs-partner decision for social impact. When to internalize capabilities vs. partner with NGOs. Collaboration continuum: philanthropic to transactional to integrative. NGO legitimacy & local trust.
In Depth
This is a make-vs-buy decision applied to social capability. The resource-based view says internalize capabilities that are valuable, rare, inimitable and organizationally embedded (your VRIO advantage); outsource the rest. Transaction-cost economics adds: partner unless transaction costs (search, contracting, monitoring, hold-up risk) exceed the cost of building in-house. NGOs offer legitimacy, local trust and specialized know-how a corporation cannot easily replicate. Austin's Collaboration Continuum charts how relationships deepen from philanthropic (arm's-length giving) through transactional (resource exchange) to integrative (shared mission, joint value creation).
Further Considerations
  • Principal-agent and mission-drift risks when partners have different metrics of success.
  • Reputational coupling: an NGO partner's scandal becomes your scandal.
  • The 'collaborative advantage vs. collaborative inertia' tension (Huxham).
  • Capacity-building vs. dependency: are you strengthening the partner or hollowing it out?
The Tension
⚡ Complementary capability gains vs. principal-agent risk and mismatched success metrics.

Finance

Financing & Responsibility

▾
Key Frameworks
ESG InvestingImpact InvestingBlended Finance
Core Concepts
How sustainability gets funded. Instruments: green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, social impact bonds. Cost-of-capital effects, fiduciary duty debates, additionality.
In Depth
Capital markets increasingly price sustainability. ESG investing screens or tilts portfolios on environmental, social and governance indicators; impact investing goes further, requiring intentional, measurable social/environmental returns alongside financial ones. Blended finance uses concessional or philanthropic capital (grants, first-loss tranches, guarantees) to de-risk deals and 'crowd in' commercial investors. Instruments: green bonds (use-of-proceeds earmarked), sustainability-linked loans (rate steps with KPI performance), and social impact bonds / pay-for-success (investors funded upfront, repaid by an outcome payer only if targets are met).
Further Considerations
  • Additionality: would the impact have occurred anyway? Without it, the impact claim is weak.
  • The fiduciary-duty debate: is considering ESG consistent with duty to maximize returns?
  • Impact washing and taxonomies/standards (EU Taxonomy, SFDR) to police claims.
  • The return spectrum: from finance-first (market-rate) to impact-first (concessionary) capital.
The Tension
⚡ Financial return vs. social return, do they trade off or compound? Risk of 'impact washing'.

Operationalize

Sustainable Resource Management: Circular Economy

▾
Key Frameworks
Circular Economy (Ellen MacArthur)9R FrameworkCradle-to-Cradle
Core Concepts
Linear 'take-make-dispose' vs. circular. Butterfly diagram: technical vs. biological cycles. 9R hierarchy. Industrial symbiosis, product-as-a-service, decoupling growth from resource use.
In Depth
The circular economy attacks the linear take-make-dispose model at the design stage. The Ellen MacArthur 'butterfly' separates two loops: technical cycles keep manufactured materials circulating through maintenance, reuse, refurbishment, remanufacture and recycling; biological cycles return safe nutrients to regenerate natural systems. The 9R framework ranks strategies by retained value, Refuse and Rethink (highest) down to Recycle (lowest, since it loses material quality and energy). Cradle-to-cradle designs products so every component is a nutrient for the next cycle. Industrial symbiosis turns one firm's waste into another's feedstock.
Further Considerations
  • Decoupling: separating economic growth from resource consumption (relative vs. absolute).
  • The rebound effect: efficiency gains can increase total consumption, erasing savings.
  • Product-as-a-service / performance economy: sell outcomes (light, mobility) not products.
  • Design for disassembly and material passports as enabling practices.
The Tension
⚡ Circularity is systemic, not cosmetic, it requires redesigning products, chains, and revenue models.

Operationalize

Materiality & Stakeholder Management

▾
Key Frameworks
Single vs. Double MaterialityMateriality MatrixStakeholder Salience (Mitchell/Agle/Wood)
Core Concepts
Identifying which issues matter enough to act/report on. Single (issue to firm value) vs. double (firm to society) materiality. Salience via power, legitimacy, urgency. Materiality matrix prioritization.
In Depth
Materiality decides where to focus. Single (financial) materiality asks which issues affect enterprise value, the investor's outside-in lens. Double materiality (EU CSRD) adds impact materiality: how the firm affects people and planet, the inside-out lens. The materiality matrix plots issues by importance to stakeholders (y) against importance to the business (x), prioritizing the top-right. To decide whose voice weights the assessment, Mitchell, Agle & Wood's salience model classifies stakeholders by power, legitimacy and urgency; those holding all three are 'definitive' stakeholders demanding priority.
Further Considerations
  • Dynamic materiality: issues migrate from immaterial to material over time (e.g., climate).
  • Salience classes: dormant/discretionary/demanding (1), dominant/dependent/dangerous (2), definitive (3).
  • Stakeholder engagement is a process, not a one-off survey; quality of dialogue matters.
  • Materiality feeds directly into reporting scope (Session 11).
The Tension
⚡ Investor-facing single materiality vs. outward double materiality, which lens governs strategy?

Operationalize

Sustainable Supply Chains

▾
Key Frameworks
Scope 1/2/3 EmissionsTiered Risk MappingSupplier Codes & Due DiligenceKraljic Supply Portfolio
Core Concepts
Responsibility beyond the firm's walls. Scope 3 (value chain) often dominates. Traceability, multi-tier monitoring, supplier audits, conflict minerals, living wage, mandated due diligence.
In Depth
Responsibility extends across the value chain, where most impact and risk actually sit. The GHG Protocol splits emissions into Scope 1 (direct, owned), Scope 2 (purchased energy) and Scope 3 (everything else up- and downstream), and Scope 3 typically dominates while being hardest to measure and control. A Kraljic-style portfolio lens classifies purchased items by supply risk and profit/strategic impact to decide where to invest scrutiny. The deeper the supplier tier, the weaker your visibility, creating an accountability gap that audits, codes of conduct, traceability tech and mandated due-diligence laws try to close.
Further Considerations
  • Resilience vs. efficiency: just-in-time minimizes cost but concentrates risk; buffering adds slack.
  • Mandatory human-rights and environmental due diligence (German LkSG, EU CSDDD).
  • Multi-tier challenge: leverage over tier-1 fades fast at tier-2, tier-3 and raw-material origin.
  • Traceability tools (blockchain, certification, supplier scorecards) and their limits.
The Tension
⚡ Resilience vs. efficiency, and the accountability gap across tiers you don't own.

Scale

Sustainability Across Business Models

▾
Key Frameworks
Typology of Sustainable Business Models (Bocken)Business-Model InnovationLeverage-Point Diagnosis
Core Concepts
Comparative session: how sustainability differs across manufacturing, services, platforms, retail, agribusiness. Same principle looks different per model. Diagnose biggest leverage & risk.
In Depth
This comparative session asks how the same principles manifest differently across business-model archetypes. Bocken et al.'s typology is the workhorse: maximize material/energy efficiency; close resource loops; substitute with renewables; deliver functionality rather than ownership; adopt a stewardship role; encourage sufficiency; repurpose for society/environment; and develop scale-up solutions. The skill is diagnosis, given a model (manufacturer, platform, retailer, agribusiness, service firm), locate the largest leverage point and the largest risk, because circularity in a product company looks nothing like it does in a service or platform business.
Further Considerations
  • Business-model innovation vs. product/process innovation, the former is harder and more durable.
  • Sufficiency-driven models confront the growth imperative head-on (selling less, better).
  • Platform models externalize impact to users/suppliers, complicating accountability.
  • Look for archetype 'mismatches': a great strategy for one model can fail in another.
The Tension
⚡ Incremental optimization vs. transformative business-model innovation.

Scale

Cross-Sector Partnerships: Challenges & Roadmap

▾
Key Frameworks
Collective Impact (Kania & Kramer)SDG 17 PartnershipsMulti-Stakeholder Governance
Core Concepts
Business + government + civil society at systemic scale. Collective impact: common agenda, shared measurement, mutually reinforcing activities, communication, backbone org. Power asymmetries.
In Depth
Cross-sector partnerships tackle problems too big for any single actor. Collective Impact (Kania & Kramer 2011) specifies five preconditions: a common agenda, shared measurement systems, mutually reinforcing activities, continuous communication, and a backbone organization to coordinate. SDG 17 frames partnership as the enabling goal for all others. The hard part is governance across actors with different time horizons, success metrics and power: business moves on quarters, government on electoral cycles, NGOs on grant cycles. Partnerships drift toward transactional convenience; the 'roadmap' challenge is building the trust and shared infrastructure that make them transformational.
Further Considerations
  • Power asymmetry: who sets the agenda, and whose metrics count, shapes outcomes.
  • Backbone organizations need neutrality and sustained, separate funding to survive.
  • Accountability diffusion: when everyone is responsible, no one is, design clear ownership.
  • Adaptive vs. technical problems: collective impact suits complex, emergent challenges.
The Tension
⚡ Collaborative advantage vs. collaborative inertia; transactional vs. transformational partnership.

Measure

Measurement & Communication

▾
Key Frameworks
GRI / SASB / ISSB / CSRDSROI & Theory of ChangeTCFD
Core Concepts
How impact is measured & reported. GRI (broad/impact), SASB (industry/financial), ISSB (investor-grade), CSRD (double materiality). SROI, logic models. Assurance.
In Depth
Measurement turns intention into accountability. The reporting landscape: GRI (broad, multi-stakeholder, impact-oriented, most widely used); SASB (industry-specific, financially material, investor-focused, now under ISSB); TCFD (climate risk in financial terms); and the ISSB's IFRS S1/S2 consolidating investor-grade standards, alongside the EU CSRD which mandates double materiality and assurance. For impact itself: theory of change and logic models trace inputs to activities to outputs to outcomes to impact, while SROI monetizes social value as a ratio. The credibility antidote to greenwashing is third-party assurance.
Further Considerations
  • Outputs vs. outcomes vs. impact: counting activities is not measuring change.
  • Attribution and the counterfactual: deadweight, displacement, drop-off in SROI.
  • Greenhushing: firms under-report to dodge scrutiny, which harms transparency.
  • Convergence: ISSB as a global baseline with jurisdictions layering on ('building blocks').
The Tension
⚡ Rigor & comparability vs. cost/complexity; greenwashing vs. 'greenhushing'; credibility via verification.

Humanize

Wrap-Up: Anchoring Sustainability to a Humanistic Lens

▾
Key Frameworks
Humanistic ManagementEconomy of the Common GoodPurpose-Driven Enterprise
Core Concepts
Capstone: human dignity & common good at the center, not just profit. Firm as a community of persons serving human flourishing. Synthesis of the full course arc.
In Depth
The capstone reframes everything through humanistic management: the firm exists to serve human flourishing and the common good, not merely to maximize shareholder value or even to balance stakeholders instrumentally. This connects to IPADE's tradition, to integral human development, and to the 'economy of the common good.' Human dignity is treated as non-negotiable and prior to profit, profit is necessary but a means, not the end. The integrative task is to trace the whole arc and argue a coherent position on the purpose of business itself.
Further Considerations
  • Means vs. ends: profit as a condition for, not the purpose of, the enterprise.
  • Subsidiarity and solidarity as organizing principles for the firm as a community of persons.
  • Contrast with pure shareholder-value and even with instrumental stakeholder theory.
  • Be ready to defend a normative thesis with evidence drawn from the course's cases.
The Tension
⚡ What, ultimately, is the purpose of business? Argue a defensible position.

Karnani's Trade-off Diagram / Sessions 1 & 3

Two inverted-U curves over "socially desirable goods": Private Profits peaks at the Private Optimum; Public Interest peaks later at the Social Optimum. Three zones follow.
In plain EnglishDoing good only makes money up to a point. At first, helping society also boosts profit (win-win). Past that point, more good costs the company money, so "doing well by doing good" only works in a limited zone.
How to read it
Two inverted-U curves are plotted against the quantity of socially desirable goods a firm produces (x-axis). The lower amber curve is the firm's private profit; the higher green curve is total public interest (social welfare). Each curve rises, peaks, then falls. The profit curve peaks first, at the Private Optimum (the output a self-interested firm would choose). The public-interest curve peaks later, at the Social Optimum (what society would prefer). The gap between those two peaks defines the shaded Zone of Tradeoff.
Why it matters
It visually demolishes the slogan 'doing well by doing good.' Win-wins are real but bounded: only in the Zone of Opportunity (left of the private optimum) does producing more social good also raise profit. The diagram shows precisely where that alignment ends.
The parts
  • Zone of Opportunity: both curves still rising, more social goods means more profit AND more welfare. CSR is easy here because self-interest already points the right way.
  • Zone of Tradeoff: between the two optima, profit is now falling while public interest still rises. Doing more good genuinely costs the firm money; voluntary CSR is unreliable, so regulation, NGO pressure or self-restraint must intervene.
  • Zone of Disaster: right of the social optimum, both curves fall. Pushing 'social goods' further now harms profit and society alike (over-provision, waste, unintended damage).
Exam use
Use this as the sharpest counter-argument to Porter & Kramer's Creating Shared Value. If asked to critique CSV or 'win-win' sustainability, cite Karnani: CSV generalizes from the Zone of Opportunity and ignores the Tradeoff and Disaster zones where markets alone fail.

Supply Portfolio Matrix (Kraljic) / Session 8

Classifies purchased items by supply risk vs. profit/strategic impact to target sustainability scrutiny.
In plain EnglishA way to sort the things you buy by how risky and how important they are. Focus your effort on the risky, important stuff; don't sweat the cheap, easy items.
How to read it
A 2×2 grid. The horizontal axis is the item's profit / strategic impact (how much it matters to the business); the vertical axis is supply risk (scarcity, few suppliers, fragility). Every purchased input falls into one of four quadrants depending on where it sits on those two axes.
Why it matters
You cannot apply equal sustainability scrutiny to every one of thousands of purchased items. The matrix tells you where to concentrate effort, high-risk, high-impact inputs deserve deep supplier partnerships, while low-stakes items can be simplified or automated.
The parts
  • Strategic (high risk, high impact): partner deeply, co-invest, embed ESG and due-diligence, these define your sustainability exposure.
  • Bottleneck (high risk, low impact): secure supply and find alternatives; risk of disruption outweighs their spend.
  • Leverage (low risk, high impact): you hold the power, use competition and buying leverage to demand better standards.
  • Non-critical (low risk, low impact): simplify, standardize, automate; minimal oversight needed.
Exam use
Pair it with Scope 1/2/3: the Strategic quadrant is usually where your biggest Scope 3 emissions and human-rights risks live, so that is where supplier codes, audits and traceability pay off most.

Materiality Matrix / Session 7

Prioritize issues in the top-right: high importance to both stakeholders and the business.
In plain EnglishA way to decide which issues actually matter. Plot each by how much it matters to the business and to people, the ones high on both are what you act on.
How to read it
A scatter/priority grid. The horizontal axis is an issue's importance to the business (effect on enterprise value); the vertical axis is its importance to stakeholders (society's concern). Each sustainability issue is plotted as a dot; the highlighted top-right box is the priority zone.
Why it matters
Firms face dozens of possible ESG issues but limited attention and reporting capacity. Materiality assessment decides which issues are 'material' enough to act on and disclose, focusing strategy where business and stakeholder concern overlap.
The parts
  • Top-right (prioritize): high to both, these drive strategy and reporting (e.g., climate, labor & human rights).
  • Top-left: stakeholders care, business impact lower, monitor and engage; reputational risk can escalate them.
  • Bottom-right: matters to the business but not yet to stakeholders, manage operationally.
  • Bottom-left: low to both, minimal attention.
Exam use
Connect to single vs. double materiality: the x-axis alone is 'single' (financial) materiality; adding the y-axis (impact on society) makes it 'double' materiality, which the EU CSRD requires. Note dynamic materiality, issues migrate toward the top-right over time.

Stakeholder Salience / Session 7

Mitchell, Agle & Wood: stakeholders holding all three attributes are "definitive".
In plain EnglishThree circles: power, legitimacy, urgency. The more of these a group has, the more attention they need. All three = top priority.
How to read it
Three overlapping circles (a Venn diagram) representing the three attributes a stakeholder can hold: Power (ability to impose its will), Legitimacy (its claim is socially valid/appropriate), and Urgency (the claim is time-sensitive and important). Where all three overlap sits the Definitive stakeholder.
Why it matters
Managers can't treat every stakeholder identically. Salience (Mitchell, Agle & Wood) ranks stakeholders by how many attributes they hold, telling managers whose claims demand priority attention right now.
The parts
  • One attribute (latent): dormant (power only), discretionary (legitimacy only), demanding (urgency only), low salience.
  • Two attributes (expectant): dominant (power+legitimacy), dependent (legitimacy+urgency), dangerous (power+urgency), moderate salience.
  • Three attributes: Definitive, highest priority; managers must respond immediately.
  • Attributes are not fixed: a stakeholder can gain urgency or build a coalition for power, moving toward the center.
Exam use
Use it to justify whose voice weights a materiality assessment (Session 7 link) and which stakeholders to engage first in a partnership (Session 10). 'Dangerous' stakeholders (power + urgency, no legitimacy) explain protests or boycotts.

The 9R Circularity Ladder / Session 6

Higher rungs retain more value. Recycling is the last resort.
In plain EnglishA ranking of ways to cut waste, best at the top. Refusing or reducing beats reusing, which beats recycling. Recycling is the last resort, not the goal.
How to read it
A descending ladder of nine strategies, widest (most valuable) at the top, narrowest at the bottom. Each rung is a way to keep materials and products in use; the higher the rung, the more economic and environmental value is retained and the less energy/material is lost.
Why it matters
Not all 'circular' actions are equal, recycling is often treated as the goal, but it sits near the bottom because it degrades material quality and consumes energy. The ladder reframes circularity as a hierarchy: prevent and slow loops before you close them.
The parts
  • Smarter use/manufacture (top): Refuse, Rethink, Reduce, avoid or minimize material need entirely; highest value.
  • Extend product lifespan (middle): Reuse, Repair, Refurbish, Remanufacture, keep the product itself circulating.
  • Useful application of materials (bottom): Repurpose, Recycle, recover materials only when the product can't be kept whole; lowest value, last resort.
Exam use
Tie to the Ellen MacArthur butterfly diagram (technical vs. biological cycles) and to the rebound effect, efficiency at low rungs can increase total consumption. A strong answer argues for designing products to enable the higher rungs (design for disassembly, product-as-a-service).
