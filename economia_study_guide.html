01 Economic Policy FOMC · Tariff cases ▾
Objectives of economic policy
The three targets policy steers toward: GDP growth, price stability (controlled inflation), and employment. Instruments push the economy toward Potential GDP or cool it down, always "with side effects."
Exam linkQuestion 2 lists these three as the definition; Question 4 and 6 are about the instruments used to hit them.
Potential GDP & the business cycle
Potential GDP is the maximum sustainable output at full employment. The gap between actual and potential output defines the business cycle. Policy boosts growth in downturns or cools an overheating economy.
Inflation FOMC
When the same money buys less over time. Split into headline vs core (core strips volatile food and energy). Expected inflation is the forward rate built from risk premiums, real rates, safe-asset yields, swaps, and sentiment surveys.
Exam linkQuestion 1 is the pure definition: prices up means money worth less, which is inflation.
Real interest rate FOMC
The nominal rate (Fed funds plus premium) adjusted for inflation. The decision is whether borrowing is expensive or cheap in real terms when timing an expansion now versus later.
Exam linkCore to Question 5: timing a fixed-rate loan against the inflation path.
AD–AS model FOMC / Tariff
Framework for shocks. The Fed can influence the demand side but cannot fix supply-chain or energy shocks (supply side).
Quantity Theory of Money (MV = PY) FOMC
Explains persistent inflation: if money grows faster than output, inflation follows. AD–AS explains the shock; MV = PY explains the persistence.
Fiscal vs monetary policy
Fiscal = government spending and taxes. Monetary = central bank moving the reference rate and money supply. Expansionary fiscal = more spending or lower taxes; contractionary = the reverse.
Exam linkQuestion 3 (which is NOT expansionary fiscal), Question 4 (cooling tool must be a central-bank tool, not a fiscal one), Question 6.
Fed's dual mandate FOMC
Balancing price stability and employment. The dilemma is acting too late or too early, and whether to hold or move the rate by 25, 50, or 75 bps. Introduces self-fulfilling prophecies in expectations and sticky inflation.
02 Balance of Payments US Current Account Deficit case ▾
Current account
Records trade and income flows. A deficit means the country is a net borrower from the rest of the world, funding investment and consumption beyond national income.
Trade balance (X − M)
The key insight: a trade deficit is an accounting manifestation of the domestic saving-investment imbalance, not its cause.
Balance of Payments & Financial Account
Where the imbalance shows up. The Financial Account records Assets (outflow of domestic savings) and Liabilities (inflow of external savings).
Net International Investment Position (NIIP)
A country's balance sheet: foreign assets held by residents minus liabilities to non-residents. A negative NIIP signals a debtor nation.
Debt sustainability
External financing must fund productive investment (generating growth that sustains trust), not just consumption. Otherwise deficits pile up liabilities and raise crisis risk.
03 Labor & Social Protection France: Modernizing or Dismantling ▾
Structural reforms
Measures that change the institutional rules of the game to raise Potential GDP. Used against productivity stagnation, high structural unemployment, high debt, or demographic imbalance. They may cost short-term stability for long-term gains.
Exam linkQuestion 10 turns on exactly this trade-off.
Welfare state vs private-sector-led model
In a private-sector-led model growth is the engine of wellbeing. In a welfare-state model growth is the financial backbone that funds redistribution through tax revenue. Both ultimately depend on growth.
Exam linkThis distinction is the whole point of Question 10's correct answer.
Labor market segmentation
Workforce (ages 15 to 65) splits into Economically Active vs Non-active, occupied vs unoccupied. Watch the unemployment trap and low-wage trap where transfers distort job-seeking, plus informality and underemployment.
Social security trilemma
Balancing coverage (how many), adequacy (benefit level), and financial sustainability.
Dependency ratio
The tax burden carried by younger workers. The striking figure: roughly 20.6M young workers financing benefits for roughly 43M, worsened by ageing. Spending that does not raise productivity becomes future taxes, debt, or weaker consumption.
04 Macro-themed Investing Global Asset Allocation case ▾
Asset allocation
How investors such as pension funds distribute capital across asset classes given debt and uncertainty (framed through "Chen's portfolio").
Yield curve & inversion
A normal curve slopes up (longer duration, higher yield). The term premium is the extra yield for holding long term, approximated by the 10-year minus 2-year spread. An inverted curve reflects pessimism and turns negative before every recession.
Fixed income: T-Bills & T-Bonds
The short end reflects the current cost of money; longer terms reflect the expected cost of money plus risk.
Expansionary policy to liquidity
More market liquidity raises risk appetite and valuations.
05 Trade Uncertainty Tariff Trouble: Caterpillar ▾
Tariff
A tax on imports. Government is one of the biggest winners (tax revenue). The traditional model is contrasted with reality, where firms depend on imported inputs.
Tax incidence
Who legally pays a tariff is not who economically bears it. Empirics cited: short-term demand contraction (+10 bps unemployment and −10 bps inflation per +1pp tariff), then a medium-term negative supply shock from costlier inputs.
Global value chains
Modern tariffs hit inputs, exporters, and fragmented chains, blurring domestic vs foreign and producer vs consumer. Caterpillar is American but economically global. Includes retaliation risk and the reshoring decision.
06 Currency Exposure Dollar, Debt & Destiny: Marfrig ▾
Reserve currency & safe haven
A currency trusted internationally for liquidity, trust, institutional credibility, and safe assets, not only economic size. A country can be large without being the preferred reserve asset.
Money supply to exchange rate
Expansionary monetary policy raises the supply of a currency; a relative oversupply depreciates it in the short term, holding other factors constant.
Exam linkThis is the entire mechanism behind Question 7.
Triffin dilemma
The world needs the US to issue debt to supply dollar liquidity, but rising debt can eventually undermine confidence in the dollar itself.
Yield to Maturity & DXY
YTM drives refinancing risk (what if investors demand higher yields). The DXY reflects confidence and capital flows as much as trade. Reserve transitions happen structurally; firms finance tactically.
07 Institutions & Transition Estonia · economic way of thinking ▾
Institutions (economic way of thinking)
Not organizations or agencies. Institutions are the humanly devised constraints that structure economic action, the rules of the game (North's definition).
Exam linkQuestion 11 tests this directly, with "formal organizations" as the trap.
Currency board & exchange-rate regimes
A monetary tool for currency stability: pegging the domestic currency to a hard anchor. Estonia pegged the kroon to the German mark. Distinguish from fiscal moves (flat tax) and structural moves (privatization, e-Residency).
Exam linkQuestion 8 asks which Estonian policy is specifically monetary.
Nominal vs real growth
Real growth strips out inflation. As an approximation, real ≈ nominal − inflation. If nominal growth is below inflation, real growth is negative even though the nominal figure is positive.
Exam linkQuestion 9: 17% nominal vs 20% inflation means negative real growth.
08 Development, Transition & Growth Washington Consensus · China · Productivity ▾
These three are pages in your Economia notebook that I could see listed but could not open in the browser. The entries below are built from standard course content for each topic, so treat them as a scaffold to check against your own notes rather than a transcription.
Washington Consensus
The market-reform policy package promoted for developing economies in the late twentieth century. Its prescriptions cluster around fiscal discipline (control deficits), tax reform (broaden the base, lower marginal rates), market-determined interest and exchange rates, trade and capital-account liberalization, privatization of state enterprises, deregulation, and secure property rights. The standard critique: it underweighted institutions, sequencing, and social protection, so identical reforms produced very different outcomes across countries.
China growth model
A state-directed, investment- and export-led model: high savings and investment rates, gradual and partial market liberalization, a large state-owned-enterprise sector, and a managed exchange rate that supported export competitiveness. The live debate is the transition from investment and exports toward domestic consumption, against headwinds of debt, demographics, and productivity slowdown. Connects to the trade and currency cases (global value chains, reserve dynamics).
Productivity
Output per unit of input, the ultimate engine of long-run growth and rising living standards. Raising potential GDP sustainably comes from productivity, which is why structural reforms target it even at short-term cost. Ties directly to the France case and to exam Question 10: both private-sector-led and welfare-state models depend on productivity growth to fund well-being.
Exam linkQuestion 10 rests on this; the long-run logic is productivity, not redistribution alone.
