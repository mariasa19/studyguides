Part 1 · Frame the Situation

The Two Modes & the GM's Job

The first sentence of any analysis: is the protagonist acting in the right mode for the situation?

Entrepreneurial
Driven by OPPORTUNITY. "What's possible?" Resources pursued regardless of what you control. Commitment is gradual, staged, revocable. Keep options open.
Traditional / Administrative
Driven by RESOURCES / BUDGET. "What can I do with what I have?" Resources allocated from what's controlled. Full commitment, planned up front. Execute the plan.
Four GM roles
1. Define business / strategy
2. Create structure
3. Execute processes
4. Align interests
Three goals
• Contribute to the common good
• Create sustainable economic value
• Build a company that survives & thrives

Most case misery comes from using the wrong mode. This is Stevenson's definition — it underlies experimentation and staged financing throughout the course.

✎ Work it through
A founder with a great opportunity insists on a full 5-year budget and headcount plan before launching. What mode are they in, and what's the risk?
They've slipped into traditional/administrative mode — committing fully and up front against a fixed plan. Under genuine uncertainty this is dangerous: it locks resources before the riskiest assumptions are tested. The entrepreneurial move is to commit gradually, keep options open, and let staged experiments (and staged financing) reveal whether the plan holds before scaling spend.
Which of the four GM roles tends to break first in an early-stage venture vs. a scaling venture?
Early stage: roles 1 (define business/strategy) and 4 (align interests — cofounders, early hires, investors). Scaling: roles 2 (create structure) and 3 (execute processes) — the company outgrows informal coordination. Diagnosing which role is failing tells you where to focus the recommendation.

Part 2 · Opportunity

The Opportunity Equation & Three-Lens Test

Dissolves the beginner's error of equating "idea" with "opportunity."

OPPORTUNITY = TARGET MARKET + PROBLEM + SOLUTION + BUSINESS MODEL "market + problem" = Is it real & large? (TAM/SAM/SOM)  |  "solution" = uniqueness  |  "business model" = how value is captured
Desirable
Will they pay? Real value created? How acute is the pain — painkiller vs. vitamin?
Feasible
Fits our capabilities and our desires. Can we actually build & deliver it?
Viable
Makes business & economic sense. Do the unit economics work?
Questions, in order
  1. Who exactly is the customer, and how acute is the pain?
  2. How big is the obtainable market (SOM), built bottom-up = units × price × realistic adoption?
  3. "Why now?" — what transition (tech / regulation / behavior) opened this?
  4. Which of the three lenses is the weak one? — concentrate the analysis there.
Worked — Rent the Runway
  • Desirable? Yes — designer wear without ownership cost; clear willingness to pay.
  • Feasible? Mostly — depends on reverse logistics & cleaning capability.
  • Viable? ← the weak lens. Hinges on rentals-per-dress (utilization), churn, fulfillment cost.
  • Why now? E-commerce trust + social-media outfit pressure + logistics maturity.
✎ Work it through
A startup claims a $50B TAM by taking "just 2% of the global coffee market." Why should this raise a red flag, and how would you re-size it?
This is top-down "% of a huge market" hand-waving — it ignores how you'd actually acquire customers. Re-size bottom-up: SOM = realistic units × price × achievable adoption given your channel, geography, and competition. A credible obtainable market is built from the customer up, not sliced from the top down.
For a new B2B AI tool, the team is sure it's desirable and feasible. Where should they spend their analysis time?
On the weak lens — viability. If desirability and feasibility are strong, the open question is whether the unit economics and business model actually capture enough value (CAC vs. LTV, gross margin after inference/compute costs, pricing power). Always direct effort to the lens most likely to break.

Part 3 · Strategy & Competitive Advantage

Winning & Sustaining It

Change the game
Reset the rules.
Solve uniquely
A way rivals can't copy.
Be unique
Hard to imitate.
Sustain it
Moats that endure.
Go first when you lock in
Economies of scale · switching costs · network effects · best locations/channels
Wait / fast-follow when
Tech changing fast · suppliers not ready · high customer-education cost (pioneer pays, you harvest)
KEEP OPTIONS OPEN → RUN MULTIPLE MODELS → COMMIT RESOURCES GRADUALLY Strategy under uncertainty = the entrepreneurial mode expressed as strategy
Worked — Gamma (AI)
Tech changes fast and the underlying model is commoditized → being first isn't the moat. Durable advantage must come from switching costs + network effects + proprietary data / workflow lock-in.
✎ Work it through
Two startups enter a brand-new market created by a tech breakthrough. One races to be first; one waits 18 months. Under what conditions is waiting the smarter call?
Waiting wins when technology is still changing rapidly (the pioneer bets on a moving target), suppliers/ecosystem aren't ready, or customer-education costs are high — the first mover spends to teach the market and the fast follower harvests educated demand at lower cost. First-mover advantage only pays if you can lock in scale, switching costs, network effects, or scarce channels.
An AI product has a slick UI but uses the same foundation model as everyone else. Where can a real moat come from?
Not from the model (commoditized). Moats come from proprietary data that compounds with usage, workflow lock-in / switching costs, network effects, and distribution. Ask: "what makes a user stay after the novelty fades?"

Part 4 · Business Model & Unit Economics

Find the 2–3 Drivers, Then Run the Math

CONTRIBUTION = Price − Variable cost  (per unit)
BREAK-EVEN = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution
LTV / CAC ≥ ~3:1 healthy  |  CAC PAYBACK = months of margin to recoup ⚠ Negative contribution can NEVER be fixed by scaling — it loses money faster. (Try the live calculator below.)
Worked — Zipcar
Unit = one car in one neighborhood. Drivers = utilization, density, fixed cost/car. Fixed cost/car high, variable/rental low → strong contribution once busy. Break-even = utilization where member-hours cover the car's fixed cost. Growth = reach density city-by-city.
✎ Work it through
A subscription box has a contribution margin of −$3 per order but the founder says "we'll fix it with volume." What do you tell them?
Volume makes it worse. Negative contribution means every incremental unit loses money, so scaling accelerates losses. The fix must come before scale: raise price, cut variable cost, or change the model so each unit is profitable. Only then does volume help cover fixed costs.
For Zipcar, why is "total members" a misleading success metric?
Because profitability is driven by local density & utilization, not aggregate membership. 100k members spread thinly leaves cars idle (fixed cost bleeds); a dense cluster pushes one car past break-even. Each neighborhood is its own break-even bet — density is the real driver.

Part 5 · Evidence & Experimentation

The Hypothesis-Driven Loop

1
Identify the RISKIEST assumption
↓
2
Write a FALSIFIABLE hypothesis: "If we do X, then ≥Y% of [sample] will Z"
↓
3
Define the METRIC + FAILURE CRITERION — before running it
↓
4
Build smallest MVP; measure on a representative sample
↓
5
PERSEVERE  ← learn →  PIVOT

Bias guards: confirmation bias, false positives, false negatives. Testing hierarchy: (1) biggest risk first, (2) problem before solution, (3) respect dependencies.

Worked — Dropbox
Riskiest assumption wasn't "can we build sync" — it was "do enough people want it & can we acquire them cheaply." Demo video tested the value hypothesis (no finished product). Referral program tested the growth hypothesis. Sign-up surge = validated learning → scale.
✎ Work it through
You're about to spend 9 months building a full product. Describe a cheaper experiment to test demand first, and define its pass/fail criterion.
Build an MVP that tests the riskiest assumption (demand) — e.g., a landing page or explainer video with a sign-up/pre-order button (Dropbox's move), or a manual "concierge" version. Decide the metric and failure criterion up front: e.g., "≥15% of 1,000 targeted visitors leave an email" = persevere; below that = pivot. Defining failure first prevents confirmation bias and vanity metrics.
Why test the problem before the solution?
If the problem isn't real or acute, no solution matters — you'd be optimizing something nobody needs. Validating the problem first is cheaper and prevents building a beautiful answer to a non-question. It also respects dependencies: solution tests assume a confirmed problem.

Part 6 · Team, Governance & Equity

Solo vs. Co-Founder & the Day-One Agreement

Q1
Do you need complementary skills/capacity you can't buy or hire fast enough?
↓ if yes
Q2
Will a co-founder add more value than the control/equity you give up?
↓ if yes
✓
CO-FOUND — and sign the agreement on day one.
Founder-agreement checklist (DAY ONE)
Vision alignment · clear roles & decision rights · equity structure · vesting (earn over time) · conflict-resolution mechanism.
Equity: equal split vs. slice-and-pie (dynamic, by weekly contribution).  Protection: colleagues → less; family/friends → MORE.
Worked — To Found or to Cofound?
Run the tree (skills gap? control preference? net value of a partner?). The real lesson: the governance / vesting / equity agreement on day one — and that friends & family need more formal structure, not less.
✎ Work it through
Two friends split equity 50/50 with no vesting. One leaves after 4 months. What goes wrong, and what should they have done?
The departed founder keeps 50% of the company for 4 months of work — a "dead-equity" cap-table problem that can sink future fundraising and demotivate the remaining founder. The fix: a vesting schedule (e.g., 4-year vest, 1-year cliff) so equity is earned over time, plus a clear founder agreement. Counter-intuitively, friends need more structure because the relationship tempts them to skip the paperwork.
When is staying solo the right call even if a co-founder would add capacity?
When you can buy or hire the missing capability instead, and when control matters more to you than the extra value a partner brings (the "rich vs. king" trade-off). If the co-founder doesn't add more value than the equity/control you give up, don't co-found.

Part 7 · Financing

The Staircase, the Math & the Terms

Bootstrap → F&F → Angel → Seed → Series A → B → C → Exit Debt rarely fits early — too risky to service; equity dominates
Rule of thumb / round
Dilution 15–20% · interval 12–18 mo · value step-up 2–3× · raise the minimum that maximizes the next milestone's value
The math
POST = PRE + INVESTMENT
INVESTOR % = INVESTMENT ÷ POST
Every new share dilutes existing holders
SAFE notes
Converts at next priced round. Cap = max conversion price (protects early $). Discount = % off the round price. Investor takes whichever is better. ⚠ Stacked SAFEs convert later, often at the cap → hidden dilution. Model conversion forward.
Terms beyond valuation = control & downside
Liquidation preference (1× non-participating vs. participating) · option pool (carved from pre-money = quiet founder dilution) · board seats / protective provisions · anti-dilution · pro-rata · vesting. A lower valuation with clean terms can beat a high valuation with bad ones.
Worked — ELCA's Series A
Build the post-money cap table, check dilution vs. 15–20%, convert any SAFE at its cap/discount, then weigh liquidation preference & board terms. Negotiate with a clear BATNA / reservation price / ZOPA, trading low-value terms for control-critical ones.
✎ Work it through
An investor offers a $20M valuation with a 2× participating liquidation preference, vs. another at $14M with 1× non-participating. In a modest $30M exit, which founder does better — and what's the lesson?
The 2× participating preference means the investor takes 2× their money off the top and then also shares in the rest — in a modest exit that can leave founders with far less despite the higher headline valuation. The $14M / 1× non-participating deal often pays founders more in small/medium exits. Lesson: headline valuation ≠ outcome; terms that govern the downside and control frequently matter more. (Use the dilution calculator to feel the ownership side.)
Why "raise the minimum that maximizes the next milestone"?
Each round should buy you to the next value-inflection point and no further. Raising more than needed dilutes you at today's (lower) valuation; raising too little risks missing the milestone that justifies the next up-round. Match the raise to the milestone to minimize dilution across the whole staircase.

Part 8 · Scaling

Should-Grow × Can-Grow

CAN GROW — low
CAN GROW — high
SHOULD GROW — high
⚠ RUNNING HOT
Build capability fast or pace deliberately.
✓ SCALE NOW
Press the advantage.
SHOULD GROW — low
✋ DON'T
No reason to grow.
Build, but…
find the strategic reason first.
EXPLORATION (flexible, opportunity-led)  →  EXECUTION (structured systems, process, control) = back to GM roles #2 (structure) and #3 (process)
Worked — CloudFlare "Running Hot?"
Demand (should-grow) high; question is whether capability/controls (can-grow) keep up — the top-left danger quadrant. Pace deliberately or invest ahead. Warning signs: rising defects/churn, firefighting, service degradation.
Worked — Duolingo
Should-grow is real, but the discipline is focus — choose the next growth vector (new subjects / monetization) while protecting the core engagement loop (the streak).
✎ Work it through
A company has huge demand but support tickets are exploding, churn is rising, and the team is firefighting. Which quadrant are they in and what are the two real options?
Top-left: "Running Hot" — high should-grow, low can-grow. The rising churn/defects/firefighting are the classic warning signs. Two options: (1) invest ahead in capabilities, processes & control systems to lift can-grow, or (2) deliberately pace growth to what the org can absorb. Ignoring it erodes the quality the business depends on.
Why might a high-growth company deliberately say "no" to an attractive new market?
Because focus compounds. Strategic scaling means protecting the core engagement/economic engine (Duolingo's streak) rather than diluting attention and resources across many vectors. The cost of lost focus — weaker core retention, scattered execution — can exceed the upside of the new market.

Part 9 · The Decision

How to Close Any Case

1. Recommendation
The specific action.
2. Trade-off accepted
Every good choice sacrifices something (rich vs. king, speed vs. control, focus vs. optionality). Name it.
3. Leading indicators
What tells you you're right — or need to pivot.
"Given [mode + opportunity diagnosis], I recommend [action], because the unit economics / strategy show [insight]. This accepts [trade-off]. I'd monitor [metric] and pivot if [failure criterion]."

S3 Capptu — choosing a wedge ▸

Apr 14 · Alternative paths to value

Case
Capptu (eC 01). Note: Business Model Analysis for Entrepreneurs. Article: Do Entrepreneurs Need a Strategy?
Technical note / framework
Business Model Analysis decomposes value prop / resources&activities / revenue model / cost structure and checks internal consistency. Do Entrepreneurs Need a Strategy? — yes: a guiding hypothesis of how you will win beats pure opportunism.
Lecture concept applied
With scarce resources you must choose a wedge and sequence the rest. Lay 2–3 candidate models side by side; pick the internally coherent, viable one.
✎ Practice & thought prompts
Capptu can sell to brands, agencies, and individuals. Why not pursue all three at once?
Each path needs different activities, customers, and resources; pursuing all dilutes focus and burns scarce capital. Choose the most desirable + feasible + viable wedge first, prove it, then sequence the others — a guiding strategy, not opportunism.
What does “internal consistency” of a business model mean here?
The pieces (value prop, required activities, revenue model, costs) must reinforce each other rather than fight. E.g., a high-touch curation promise paired with a low take-rate, high-volume model is inconsistent — the economics will not support the promise.
S4 Zipcar — utilization & density ▸

Apr 22 · Business model design & unit economics

Case
Zipcar + Study Questions Zipcar. Note: Note on Business Model Analysis for the Entrepreneur.
Technical note / framework
Unit economics: isolate the repeatable unit, then contribution = price − variable cost; break-even = fixed ÷ contribution; check CAC/LTV and payback. Negative contribution cannot be scaled away.
Lecture concept applied
Unit = one car in one neighborhood. Drivers: utilization, density, fixed cost/car. Each market is its own break-even bet; growth = reach density city-by-city.
✎ Practice & thought prompts
Define the “unit” and the 2–3 driving variables for Zipcar.
Unit = one car in one neighborhood. Drivers = utilization (member-hours/car), local density (members per area), and fixed cost per car. Profit flips positive once utilization passes the break-even threshold.
At what point does adding a car in a new neighborhood make sense?
When projected local density will drive utilization past the point where contribution (price − variable cost) × rental-hours covers that car’s fixed cost. Below that, the car bleeds fixed cost — so density must come first.

S6 To Found or to Cofound? — team & equity ▸

To Found or to Cofound? + Study Questions.

Founder’s dilemmas: solo vs. co-founder; complementary skills & shared risk vs. dilution, control loss, relationship risk. Governance/vesting/equity set early.

S8 GenapSys — model choice & cash ▸

May 15 · Business models, cash flow & financing

Case
HBS 9-814-050 GenapSys + Study Questions + financial model (XLS).
Technical note / framework
Profit vs. cash; burn rate & runway; matching financing to stage/risk. Model choice (razor/razor-blade vs. alternatives) drives cash needs.
Lecture concept applied
In deep-tech, model choice and financing are inseparable. Translate the model into a cash-flow forecast; profit on paper means nothing if cash timing kills you.
✎ Practice & thought prompts
Why can a profitable company still run out of cash?
Profit is accounting (revenue recognized minus matched expenses); cash is the bank balance. Upfront equipment/inventory, late-paying customers, and growth all consume cash before profit converts. Watch burn rate and runway (cash ÷ burn).
How does a hardware + consumables (razor/razor-blade) model change financing needs vs. selling instruments outright?
Placing instruments cheaply to earn recurring consumables defers revenue and increases upfront cash needs (you fund the hardware now, earn later) — demanding more/earlier financing than an outright-sale model. Model choice sets the size and timing of the raise.

S10 ELCA + SAFE conversion ▸

May 25 · Financing (cont.) — SAFEs

Case
HBS 9-823-079 ELCA (cont.). Note: SAFE Notes: An Introduction + model (XLS).
Technical note / framework
SAFE = convert-later instrument (not debt). Cap = max conversion valuation; discount = % off the round price; investor takes the better. Stacked SAFEs create hidden future dilution.
Lecture concept applied
SAFEs are fast and founder-friendly but bite later — model the conversion forward into the post-Series-A cap table.
✎ Practice & thought prompts
A SAFE has a $5M cap and the priced round is at $10M post-money. Roughly how does the SAFE holder benefit?
They convert as if the company were worth the $5M cap, not $10M — effectively buying shares at half the round price, so they get ~2× the equity their dollars would otherwise buy. That upside is founder dilution realized later.
Why is “a SAFE is free money now” a dangerous way to think?
Because the dilution is deferred and stacked — several SAFEs all convert at the next round (often at their caps) on top of the new investor’s stake, diluting founders far more than they intuit. Always model conversion before signing.
S11 Medical-Industry AI (A–E) + VC workshop ▸

May 26 · Negotiating with founders & investors

Case
Reading: Partnering with Venture Capitalists. Case: CiiE 23 eC 04 (A–E) Entrepreneurship in the Medical Industry. Note: Terms Relevant to Risky Financing Ecosystems. Workshop: VC Negotiation (PPTX).
Technical note / framework
VC incentives (power-law/portfolio, need for exits) shape terms. Term-sheet vocabulary: liquidation preference, option pool, board seats, protective provisions, anti-dilution. Prepare with BATNA / reservation price / ZOPA.
Lecture concept applied
Founder–investor negotiation is about more than valuation. Trade low-value terms to win control-critical ones; valuation vs. control is the central tension.
✎ Practice & thought prompts
You enter a raise with no alternative offer. How does that weaken you, and what should you build first?
With no BATNA (best alternative), your walk-away power is near zero, so you must accept worse terms. Before negotiating, create alternatives (other investors, a path to bootstrap longer) to widen the ZOPA in your favor and set a real reservation price.
Why might a founder concede on valuation to win on board control?
Because control (board seats, protective provisions) governs the company’s direction and the founder’s ability to execute. A slightly lower valuation with a founder-friendly board can be worth more than a high valuation that hands investors decision rights.

S13 CloudFlare — “Running Hot?” ▸

Jun 1 · Operational scaling

Case
HBS 9-813-145 CloudFlare, Inc.: Running Hot? Note: Scaling a Startup: Pacing Issues. Reading: Leading High-Growth Ventures.
Technical note / framework
Pace growth to what the organization can absorb. Too fast breaks quality, culture, control; too slow loses the market. Watch early warning signs of “running hot.”
Lecture concept applied
Build repeatable processes and control systems ahead of the curve; the leader transitions from doing the work to building the organization that does it.
✎ Practice & thought prompts
What are the early warning signs that a company is “running hot”?
Rising defect/churn rates, constant firefighting, deteriorating service/quality, and unit economics that held at small scale starting to slip (worse customers, rushed hiring). They signal can-grow is lagging should-grow.
How must the founder’s role change to scale successfully?
From doing the work to building the organization — delegation, hiring ahead of need, installing control/information systems, and shaping culture intentionally. What works at 20 people sinks the company at 200 without this transition.

HBS 9-825-097 Duolingo: On a “Streak” + Study Questions.
Technical note / framework
Strategic scaling: choose the next growth vector (geography, segment, product, monetization) while protecting the core engagement loop. Focus vs. expansion trade-off.
Lecture concept applied
Strategic scaling means saying no to attractive distractions and doubling down on what compounds — protect the streak (retention engine) while extending it.
✎ Practice & thought prompts
How should Duolingo decide its next growth vector?
Evaluate each option (new subjects, monetization, gamification) on whether it protects or strengthens the core engagement loop (the streak) that drives retention, versus diluting it. Prioritize vectors that compound the core, not just add surface area.
What is the cost of losing focus during scaling?
Weaker core retention, scattered execution, and resources spread too thin to win anywhere. The upside of a new market can be outweighed by erosion of the engine that made the company work.

Lecture concept applied

✎ Practice & thought prompts
How does the leader’s job change from idea to scale?
Early: define the opportunity and align the first team (roles 1 & 4). Later: build structure and process and lead a growing organization (roles 2 & 3), eventually transitioning from founder-doer to CEO-builder, with succession and culture as the venture matures.
What single discipline carries through every stage of the course?
Reducing uncertainty through staged commitment — opportunity-led, hypothesis-driven, gradual resource commitment (the entrepreneurial mode), whether you are testing demand, raising a round, or scaling.
